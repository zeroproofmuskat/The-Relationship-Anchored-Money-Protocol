# The Relationship-Anchored Money Protocol: A Third-Generation Monetary System Anchored in Exchange

## Abstract

This paper proposes a new monetary protocol whose value is anchored neither in scarcity nor in the credit promise of any external institution, but in the real exchange of goods and services occurring within the system itself.

The protocol operates through two parallel core mechanisms. First, relational transparency: all value transfers are publicly verifiable on-chain, and every transfer carries a structural cost, a 20% transaction tax, ensuring that social liabilities generated by economic activity cannot be anonymously hidden or passed on without cost. Second, redistribution: the full proceeds of the transaction tax fund a universal basic income, or UBI, automatically channeling value created by economic activity back to every participant. These two mechanisms are encoded directly into the transfer rules of the currency itself. They are automatic consequences of how the money moves, not policies enforced by an external authority — forming the basis of a self-contained economic system independent of any external currency.

This protocol is more than a new form of money. By eliminating financial speculation, the 20% transaction tax also eliminates the economic basis for financial intermediary power and credit cycles, giving rise to a new mode of economic organization built on direct, transparent distribution. It is an entirely different financial species: value anchored in real exchange relationships rather than in freely transferable securities detached from those relationships. It is a third answer to the question of what money should be.

The protocol is designed for deployment as a set of smart contracts on an Ethereum L2 network, with ETH as the sole reserve asset and the only interface between this system and the external monetary world.

This whitepaper is published anonymously, without accompanying code or a deployment plan. It attempts to offer the blockchain community , after eighteen years of speculative cycles and narrative disillusionment, an answer to the question: what is this technology actually for?

If this answer withstands scrutiny, its implementation should emerge organically from researchers and developers who find it compelling. Its growth, and the economic and social transformation it triggers, requires no one to execute it; it only requires individuals acting on their own judgment of where their interests lie.

## Chapter 1: Two Axioms

The entire protocol rests on two foundational claims about the nature of economic activity. Every design decision that follows, from the 20% tax to UBI to the prohibition of lending to identity verification, is derived step by step from these two axioms.

### 1.1 First Axiom: Value Exists in Real Exchange Relationships

The essence of an economy is the ongoing exchange of value between people. One person does something for another; the other reciprocates. A baker bakes bread for a customer. A doctor treats a patient. A teacher instructs a student. Value is born in these real exchange relationships, and it exists only within them. Detached from any relationship, it is just a number hanging in the air.

These economic activities generate demand for a unit of account, something to measure and price the exchange. Money was originally just a symbol for recording these relationships. This step, call it symbolization, is necessary and useful. But from the very beginning, human money performed a second operation simultaneously: securitization. It detached the symbol from the relationship it recorded, turning it into a standalone, freely transferable instrument, a security.

Conventional finance treats "money" and "securities" as fundamentally different things. Money is a general medium of exchange; securities are tradable financial instruments. But this distinction obscures a deeper fact: money itself is humanity's oldest and most fundamental security. It packages real exchange relationships and social trust into an instrument that can circulate freely, detached from the relationships that gave rise to it. This is structurally identical to packaging loans into tradable bonds. It simply happens at a deeper, older, and therefore less visible layer.

And the underlying asset being securitized, social trust, the basic willingness of people to cooperate and exchange, has been entirely excluded from the analytical framework of economics. Economics analyzes assets, liabilities, cash flows, and risk, but never analyzes the precondition that makes all economic activity possible: social trust. This is a structural blind spot. Precisely because money securitized social trust and laundered it into anonymous digits, the underlying asset vanished from view.

Securitization produces a profound consequence: it allows the social liabilities generated by economic activity to be hidden. A person can exploit trust in an exchange, extract disproportionate value, and damage real relationships; but they walk away with money, and money is anonymous, decoupled from its source. Money launders its origins. Looking at the numbers in someone's wallet, you cannot distinguish whether those numbers represent genuine value they created for others or value they extracted from others. Money packages all acquisition, whether honest or parasitic, into the same clean, freely transferable token. The damaged relationships stay behind, permanently severed from the money.

This is structurally identical to financial securitization: packaging potentially impaired underlying assets, toxic relationships and overdrawn trust, into a clean-looking security that circulates freely, shielding the holder from the bad debt underneath. Financial securitization hides impaired financial assets. Monetary securitization hides impaired social relationships. The subprime crisis was simply the original securitization pattern of money itself, stacked several more layers deep in the financial domain.

If you accept that value exists in real exchange relationships, then sound money should do one thing: preserve symbolization, because recording exchanges with numbers is necessary, while blocking securitization, preventing those numbers from detaching from relationships and circulating independently, so that social liabilities have nowhere to hide. Such money makes the numbers in every person's wallet a reflection of their real social balance sheet, their wealth speaks to who they are: their effort, their skill, the value they have created for others.

### 1.2 Second Axiom: People Are the Purpose of Economic Activity

What is an economy for? The answer to this question determines everything. In prevailing economic thinking, people are a factor of production, labor, human capital, a variable in a production function. A person's worth is defined by their contribution to output, by their utility as an input.

The cruelty of this framing is hidden as long as the economy runs smoothly and human labor is still needed. But as technology advances to the point where human labor can be replaced at lower cost, the AI era we are now entering, the cruelty becomes fully exposed: if people are merely a production input, and a cheaper substitute has arrived, then people become surplus.

The second axiom rejects this framing. It holds that people are the purpose of economic activity. The entire point of an economy is to serve human well-being. Every person, simply by virtue of being a person, holds an inalienable claim on a share of the total value created by the economic system. This claim does not depend on how much they produce, whether they are "useful," or whether they can outcompete machines. It is a birthright.

If you accept that people are the purpose, then the value created by economic activity should flow back to every person: equally, unconditionally, without auditing output. This demands a mechanism that automatically, inherently, and equally distributes a portion of the value generated by economic activity to all participants.

## Chapter 2: Why Existing Money Violates Both Axioms

All existing money, historical metallic currencies, modern fiat, and post-blockchain cryptocurrencies, is securitized money as described in Chapter 1. The problems they share can be traced to a single root: they hide the social liabilities generated by economic activity, allowing bad debt to accumulate beneath the surface until it forces settlement through collapse.

### 2.1 How Securitized Money Hides Social Liabilities

When people first used shells and precious metals to record exchange, they performed symbolization and securitization simultaneously: recording the exchange and detaching the symbol for independent transfer in the same act. These two steps were never separated, so thoroughly fused that no one realized they could be.

The consequence of securitization: all forms of acquisition in economic activity, whether through genuine contribution or through extraction, are laundered into the same anonymous token. Money earned through honest business looks identical to money earned by overdrawn trust and asymmetric extraction. Money records no provenance, retains no information about the quality of the underlying relationship.

Every instance of parasitic acquisition, rent-seeking, speculation, control-point extraction, is masked by the anonymity of money. The extractor walks away with money that moves freely to new hands; the overdrawn trust, the damaged relationships, the asymmetrically extracted value remain behind as social liabilities, permanently severed from the money. The entire economy operates in a state where the underlying relationships are continuously being depleted while the surface ledger appears healthy, until accumulated social liabilities grow too large to conceal and force a violent settlement.

This is structurally identical to financial securitization: impaired underlyings, subprime loans and toxic relationships alike, are packaged into clean-looking securities, money, circulating freely in the market. Everyone sees the face value of the security. No one sees the bad debt underneath.

### 2.2 Rent-Seeking: Social Extraction Behind the Veil of Anonymity

If money does not distinguish honest acquisition from parasitic acquisition, then parasitism inevitably becomes the rational strategy. Its cost, damage to real relationships, is hidden by money's anonymity, while its benefit, money received, is indistinguishable from honest earnings.

This is the essence of rent-seeking: it is social extraction enabled by the anonymity of securitized money. Those who profit by holding existing stock, through interest or asset appreciation, derive their returns from other people's real labor and output. But this correspondence is laundered away by money's anonymity, made to look as though money breeds money on its own. Lending at interest, holding assets for appreciation, sitting at a control point in a flow of funds and taking a cut. The causal link between these acts of extraction and the social liabilities they create, heavier burdens on borrowers, diluted purchasing power, hollowed-out communities, is perfectly severed by money.

This is a position that securitized money structurally guarantees will be occupied. In a system where acquisition cannot be traced to its source, maximizing acquisition is the rational play. Whether your gains come from honest labor or from extracting others, they look the same in your wallet, and the latter is usually easier and more lucrative. Everyone is pushed toward this choice, because the system's anonymity makes extraction and genuine exchange indistinguishable, and choosing the easier path is simply rational.

For most people, participating in rent-seeking is less a choice than self-defense against being drained. Rent-seekers and those they extract from are both captives of the same securitized structure; they simply bear it in different ways.

### 2.3 The Trap of Mutual Harm

Follow the logic of rent-seeking to its conclusion and you arrive at what securitized money truly imposes on everyone: a trap of mutual harm. In a system where parasitic and honest acquisition cannot be told apart, everyone is pushed to maximize extraction, because if they don't, someone else will, and they become the target.

This is a textbook prisoner's dilemma: everyone is forced into a game of mutual extraction that nobody actually wants but nobody can unilaterally exit. Securitized money drags all participants into this game. Rent-seekers are merely those temporarily ahead. They sit on the same sinking ship as everyone else. When accumulated social liabilities become unsustainable, even the leaders get pulled under.

This also makes the moral question, "Is rent-seeking wrong?", suspect. When rent-seeking is structurally compelled, rational, and universal, moral exhortation against it is not merely impotent. It may reinforce the structure. Those who are persuaded to stop become more docile prey, and the strongest incentive to preach such morality belongs precisely to the leaders: it keeps the prey compliant and the hierarchy stable.

### 2.4 The Supply-Demand Gap and Pervasive Scarcity Anxiety

The anonymity of securitized money allows purchasing power to drain continuously from the majority toward the few who occupy extraction positions. The extracted purchasing power settles into capital stock, exiting the real demand for goods and services. On one side, supply expands under capital's drive; on the other, demand is systematically hollowed out. Between them opens a structural chasm. This is the true root of "overproduction": purchasing power is continuously siphoned from the consumption side by anonymous extraction.

This chasm seeps into every mind, becoming a pervasive scarcity anxiety. In a system where extraction is everywhere and invisible, everyone feels that there is never enough, because their purchasing power is being drained by a force they cannot see. Saving, hoarding, chasing yield. These become existential imperatives. This anxiety is often mistaken for human nature. In reality, it is a psychological condition imposed by a monetary system that makes parasitism invisible.

### 2.5 Default at the Base Layer: Why Crises Are More Than Supply-Demand Imbalances

Combine the above, and the true nature of economic crisis comes into view. It is far more than a supply-demand mismatch.

During boom periods, the anonymity of securitized money allows every form of social extraction, from rent-seeking to speculation to control-point exploitation to trust overdraft, to accumulate unchecked, because money launders these activities clean. Everyone's books look healthy. But at the base layer, real relationships are being depleted: trust is shrinking, communities are hollowed out, purchasing power leaks from workers, and unsustainable economic structures, unpayable debts, value-free rent-seeking apparatuses, Ponzi-like speculation chains, keep piling up. These are the social liabilities hidden by money's anonymity.

When these liabilities reach a critical threshold, when trust has been overdrawn too deeply, purchasing power drained too far, unsound structures inflated too large. The base layer defaults en masse. This manifests not only as a collapse in the goods market. It erupts as bank runs, debt crises, unemployment waves, social unrest, and even revolutions and wars, because what has been overdrawn is not just purchasing power but interpersonal trust, community cohesion, and the entire relational fabric on which society runs.

The boom-bust cycle is this process repeating: accumulate hidden social liabilities → base-layer mass default → forced settlement → begin accumulating again. Each boom builds a new round of hidden liabilities. Each crash is a partial, painful, costly forced settlement. And because the securitized structure remains unchanged, accumulation resumes after every settlement, building toward the next eruption.

### 2.6 The Metallic Money Era: Acute Base-Layer Defaults

Under metallic money, whether gold standard or silver standard, the money supply was locked to the physical stock of gold. When social liabilities reached the tipping point and base-layer defaults began, the system had no buffer. Default erupted in a brutally acute, all-encompassing fashion.

The mechanism: at the onset of crisis, everyone rushes to convert everything into cash or gold, the fixed-supply, undilutable ultimate safe asset. This is a stampede of all participants toward the same quantity-locked safe haven. In this stampede, cash becomes extremely scarce; money freezes in the grip of fear. Firms and individuals desperate for liquidity to survive must borrow at extreme cost. The result is a counterintuitive but structurally inevitable phenomenon: the deeper the crisis, the higher the interest rate.

Rising rates accelerate liquidation: more firms are pushed into default and bankruptcy, forced to dump assets at fire-sale prices, collapsing asset values trigger more forced liquidation. A self-feeding death spiral. Two engines run simultaneously: rigid contagion through debt chains, where one default cascades into the next; and deflationary hoarding of hard money, where holding gold becomes the highest-return strategy as its purchasing power rises, driving frantic accumulation.

These engines, stacked together, can push a depression to multi-year, generation-destroying depths. Every major depression under metallic money was this spiral running to its end. It ran to the end because the money supply was locked by physical reality, and nothing within the system could stop it.

### 2.7 Fiat Money: Deferring Base-Layer Defaults, and the Cost of Deferral

The fiat money system evolved specifically to prevent the acute explosions described above. Its core breakthrough: the money supply is no longer locked by physical reality but can expand or contract as needed. When crisis strikes, when everyone scrambles for cash, rates spike, and the liquidation spiral ignites. The issuer can inject new money into the system, breaking the scramble for a fixed pool of cash, suppressing rates, halting cascading liquidation, and stopping the spiral. This is a genuine advance. It mitigated the unstoppable acute explosions of the metallic era.

But fiat changed nothing fundamental: money remains a security that can be freely transferred, at zero cost, detached from any real relationship. Social liabilities can still be hidden, laundered, and accumulated. What fiat added was a layer of interventionary capacity on top of this unchanged securitized nature: when base-layer defaults begin to surface, inject new money to suppress them, defer them, paper over them.

This deferral creates problems in two directions simultaneously. One direction is the distribution of the injected force: new liquidity does not flow toward real production or ordinary consumption. It floods into the assets most convenient for hoarding and appreciation, real estate, equities, financial products. Asset prices hit new highs while ordinary people's purchasing power is steadily diluted. This is the same interventionary force torn in half by securitized money's logic: fake prosperity for asset holders, real austerity for workers.

The other direction is the cost of deferral itself: every injection of new money to cover base-layer defaults draws down the issuer's own sovereign credit. Debt grows with each cycle, never shrinking. The previous round's base-layer defaults were never truly settled. Only papered over by a larger round of injection.

When accumulated overdrafts exhaust credibility, when markets no longer believe the issuer can keep papering over, all deferred base-layer defaults surface at once. Fiat converted the metallic system's acute, unstoppable base-layer explosions into chronic, credit-financed base-layer accumulation. The disease is the same; only the presentation changed from acute to chronic. The terminal outcome remains identical.

This process of hiding, deferring, and papering over base-layer defaults also produces a byproduct: ever-increasing systemic complexity. Every patch for a problem requires new institutions, new regulations, new oversight, new financial instruments. And every new layer of complexity creates new hiding places for social extraction.

The root of all this remains monetary securitization: because social liabilities can be hidden, we need an enormously complex regulatory apparatus to try to find them. Because money can be transferred at zero cost, holding and controlling it is profitable — so a vast financial intermediary layer has every incentive to make itself indispensable. A currency that makes social liabilities unhideable and makes controlling money unprofitable would need neither the intermediary layer nor the complex regulation built to search for hidden risk in the dark.

### 2.8 When the System Must Bail Out Speculation: The Terminal Logic of Fiat

Bring these threads together at a single concrete moment, and the full logic of the mechanism becomes unmistakable. The moment when accumulated social liabilities finally produce a systemic crisis, and the system reveals what it is.

Speculation is an inevitable product of securitized money. Since money is a security that can be transferred at zero cost detached from real relationships, since assets can be bid up detached from real output, and since lending provides leverage to amplify bets — then betting borrowed money on rising prices of hoardable assets becomes the highest-return strategy.

The housing bubble of the early twenty-first century was this logic taken to its extreme. The social liabilities at the base layer, massive volumes of loans that could never be repaid, were packaged layer upon layer into clean-looking securities. Speculators piled in with leverage. Everyone's books showed enormous wealth. Until the base-layer defaults began to surface, the securitized bad debt was exposed en masse, and the entire structure collapsed.

In the 2008 crisis, the fiat system did something that revealed its entire nature: it used the purchasing power of the entire society to bail out the losses of speculators. The system was forced into this by its own structure: because it was built on securitized money and rigid debt chains, the contagion risk from base-layer defaults was too large to allow speculators to bear their own losses. Using society's purchasing power to bail them out was the structurally determined, nearly inevitable outcome. Through its actions, the system enforced a rule: the gains from speculation accrue to the few; the losses from speculation are socialized. This rule is the terminal logic of fiat money itself.

## Chapter 3: Eighteen Years After Bitcoin

Before describing the third-generation money, it is worth examining Bitcoin and the eighteen years that followed. This history warrants its own chapter. Not only because it left behind a genuine technical legacy on which this protocol runs, but because its misdiagnosis of the problem set the direction for an entire movement. And that direction was wrong from the start.

### 3.1 Bitcoin's Misdiagnosis

Bitcoin was born in the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis, born from deep dissatisfaction with fiat money, and the pain behind that dissatisfaction was real. People watched the system use the purchasing power of all of society to bail out the failures of a few speculators. Their money was openly diluted, and they could do nothing about it.

But Bitcoin's diagnosis of the crisis was wrong at the most fundamental level. What was the real lesson of 2008? At its core, it was a securitization disaster, impaired mortgage loans, transparently absurd and unsustainable lending relationships, were packaged into securities, securities into derivatives, layer upon layer until they were completely detached from the underlying reality. When the impaired base layer defaulted en masse, the entire structure collapsed. This lesson was partially absorbed at the regulatory level — post-crisis rules tightened oversight of securitization. But the reflection was never pushed one layer deeper: isn't money itself the original securitization?

Bitcoin never asked this question. Its reading of 2008 was: the problem is that the issuer, the government, is unreliable and can print money at will to dilute purchasing power. So its solution was to build a currency beyond any issuer's control, absolutely scarce, impossible to inflate.

This reading blamed the issuer of the security. The real problem was securitization itself. Regardless of who issues it, regardless of whether it can be inflated, as long as money is a security that can be transferred at zero cost detached from real relationships, social liabilities can still be hidden, and every problem will recur.

### 3.2 Why Scarcity Is Wrong

The implicit message Bitcoin carried was: securitization itself is not the problem, the issuer is. Scarcity is the guarantee of value. This message was not only wrong; it set the direction for everything that followed.

Why is "scarcity equals value" wrong? Because scarcity makes something suitable as a vehicle for securitization: limited supply, hard to dilute, therefore more reliable as a security, easier to gain broad acceptance. But scarcity does not produce value in itself.

Gold outcompeted shells and cattle as the dominant vehicle for expressing value because it was scarce, durable, and hard to forge, all attributes that made it a good security. Bitcoin used mathematics to guarantee absolute scarcity, deploying extraordinary technical genius to create the most perfect security in history. But it never questioned why a security was needed in the first place.

By hardcoding supply at 21 million units, Bitcoin did block arbitrary inflation. But it simultaneously re-anchored value to a stock entirely detached from real exchange relationships — anchored to "what fraction of this fixed total do I hold." In its fundamental cognitive properties, this is the same species as gold: value derives not from any real exchange relationship but from abstract scarcity unrelated to human economic activity.

Supporters often call Bitcoin "digital gold," and this label is honest. It is indeed the gold thesis replicated in digital form. Precisely because it is decoupled from real exchange relationships, it has no real anchor for its price. Price can only be determined by what the next buyer is willing to pay, which depends on what they believe the buyer after them will pay. The entire price rests on layers of expectation with no foundation. A natural breeding ground for speculation. It ultimately became a casino because its value properties, from the very beginning, left no room for real exchange relationships.

### 3.3 How This Misdiagnosis Misdirected an Entire Movement

Bitcoin's "scarcity equals value" doctrine set the template for every cryptocurrency that followed. Nearly every subsequent token replicated the logic of "manufacture scarcity → hype scarcity → profit from scarcity", and the vast majority had nothing to offer beyond scarcity. Almost every one was a scam.

What these scams depleted was the crypto community's most critical asset: interpersonal trust. Every new coin issuer promised some form of value. Early buyers committed real money on the basis of trust. When the promise collapsed and the price cratered, what was destroyed was not just money but people's trust in each other and in the technology. In the framework of Chapter 2, these were successive rounds of base-layer social credit defaults — happening within the specific relational network of the crypto community.

Repeated collapses and scams overdrew the community's trust to a deeply negative balance: not just zero, but negative — people didn't merely stop trusting crypto; they developed active hostility toward blockchain technology itself. Public trust in a genuinely valuable technology was buried alongside the scams spawned by the false doctrine of "scarcity equals value."

### 3.4 The Same Old Ghost Consuming Every New Frontier

The eighteen years that followed were the same cycle on repeat: every new application paradigm, however beautiful its original conception, was ultimately consumed by speculation.

The first wave was the ICO frenzy: thousands of projects raised enormous sums on the strength of a whitepaper, with buyers caring only whether the token would go up. A tool that might have funded real ventures became a speculative fundraising circus at unprecedented scale. The second wave was called decentralized finance: rebuilding lending, leverage, and derivatives on-chain — rebuilding every rent-seeking component described in Chapter 2, only stripping out the banks, accelerating with code, removing the brakes, then exploding faster and more completely. The third wave was the tokenization of everything: worthless meme coins reaching multi-billion-dollar valuations on nothing more than a slogan, the entire market driven by fear of missing out rather than any real value.

The most iconic collapse of this carnival was an algorithmic stablecoin: backed by no reserves, relying solely on algorithmic arbitrage between two tokens to maintain its dollar peg, and attracting capital with an unsustainable fixed yield of 20%. When confidence wavered, the algorithm didn't stabilize the price. It ignited a death spiral. Within a week, forty to sixty billion dollars evaporated, triggering a cascade of lending platform and exchange failures.

### 3.5 Endgame: A Community That Abandoned Its Own Value Narrative

Look at the end of these eighteen years and you see something more revealing than any single collapse: the community's most vibrant applications became vehicles for importing external value onto the chain for speculative trading.

First, stablecoins, the dollar's value ported directly onto the chain, became the most successful thing in the entire new frontier. Then came tokenized equities: even stocks, commodities, and indices were brought on-chain. The most successful decentralized exchange's primary battleground became tokenized versions of NVIDIA, Tesla, and the S&P 500. More telling still, holders of these tokenized stocks typically received nothing more than a claim against the issuer. A pipe for routing external prices onto the chain for speculation.

This endgame is the clearest evidence that the community's own value narrative had gone fully bankrupt. It retreated into a purely technical role: a faster settlement rail that generates no value of its own, only transporting external value for others to speculate on.

Technical success accompanied by total narrative bankruptcy.

### 3.6 A Great Technology, and Its Necessary Disillusionment

Yet these eighteen years proved blockchain's real value.

A truly revolutionary technology, at its birth, is almost always first co-opted by old assumptions so deeply held they are mistaken for axioms, and only after being broken by them can it be reborn. Blockchain's old assumption was "value can be packaged into a freely transferable security": a belief that spans the entirety of civilized history.

People took this most powerful new tool and did the first thing they could think of: pushed the logic of securitization to extremes never before possible. And so the collapses were denser and more spectacular than any before. The eighteen-year disaster was the old assumption, riding blockchain as its most powerful vehicle ever, performing its most thorough self-exposure.

Exposure is the precondition for rebirth. Blockchain's real power lies in something else entirely: for the first time, it enables a set of rules to be faithfully executed without any trusted institution, and this is precisely the one missing piece required to block the securitization of money and re-anchor value in real relationships. The round of being broken by the old assumption was a necessary disillusionment. After disillusionment, what it can truly do finally comes into view.

## Chapter 4: How the Third-Generation Money Realizes Both Axioms

Value exists in real exchange relationships. People are the purpose of economic activity. In this system, these two axioms interlock into a self-sustaining cycle.

The first axiom generates value and prevents its securitization. Every real transaction is recognized as value creation, and 20% of it is captured as a universal transaction tax.

The tax serves a dual function: it imposes a real cost on every value transfer, making zero-cost securitized transfer structurally impossible; and it provides an absolutely reliable funding source for the second axiom.

The second axiom distributes the value created by economic activity. The value captured by the transaction tax is distributed equally to every participant as universal basic income, UBI.

Creation on the flow side, distribution on the purpose side. Every transaction on the flow side feeds the purpose side's distribution; every distribution on the purpose side returns to the flow side as new consumption and exchange. The two axioms form a closed loop through a single pivot: real exchange creates value; value flows equally back to people as the purpose; people re-enter real exchange carrying that value.

This cycle needs no external push: people must exchange to live, generating value; exchange generates tax, forming distribution funds; tax flows through UBI equally back to everyone; people receive value and go exchange again.

This is the entire design principle of the third-generation money. The relationship-anchored money proposed in this protocol. It replaces the foundational logic: preserve the symbolic property of money while blocking its securitized property, so that social liabilities have nowhere to hide. The unit of account is provisionally called "points." All subsequent references to "points" in this whitepaper denote the base unit of the relationship-anchored money.

### 4.1 Blocking Securitization: Making Every Value Transfer Carry Real Weight

The core mechanism implementing the first axiom is a single, extremely simple rule: whenever any points arrive in a real person's personal wallet, 20% is automatically deducted as a transaction tax and routed to the UBI pool. Two cases are exempt: UBI distributions and exchange-channel minting.

This 20% is a structural cost. It gives weight to every value transfer. In securitized money, value moves from hand to hand at zero cost, leaving no trace, and this is the core attribute of securitization, the reason social liabilities can be hidden. When every transfer automatically surrenders 20%, zero-cost transfer becomes structurally impossible.

Once transfer carries real, unavoidable cost, the following consequences emerge:

Hoarding produces no return. Holding points generates no yield. Just a number sitting idle, doing nothing.

Rent-seeking becomes structurally unviable. You cannot profit by sitting on existing stock, because holding does not appreciate.

Speculation becomes prohibitively expensive. Every leg of a round trip is taxed at 20%; high-frequency in-and-out strategies are bled at every turn until they are unprofitable.

Securitization-style laundering becomes extremely costly. Attempting to move value extracted through illegitimate means away from its source, to sever the link to the original relationship, costs 20% per transfer. After a few hops, almost nothing remains.

Crucially, the 20% tax flows through UBI to every participant, so this structural cost is automatically and evenly redistributed to every real person in the system. It simultaneously achieves two things: the first axiom's realization, preventing value from detaching from exchange relationships, and the second axiom's realization, returning value to every person.

### 4.2 Value Returns to People: Realizing the Second Axiom

The full 20% transaction tax is distributed equally to every identity-verified participant. This is universal basic income, UBI. It is the direct implementation of the second axiom: people are the purpose; every person, simply by existing, holds a claim on the value created by the economy.

UBI's funding source is embedded in the transfer rules of the currency itself. As long as transactions occur within the system, UBI is being generated and distributed. It requires no approval, no administration, no institution to adjudicate eligibility. It is an automatic byproduct of money in motion.

This also resolves the most anxiety-inducing question of the AI era. As more labor can be performed by machines at near-zero cost, in the securitized money framework this means people, the "input", are made redundant and lose their income source. In this system, machines replacing human labor does not mean people lose value: because people's economic rights derive not from their output but from the fact of their existence.

The output of machines is still transacted. Transactions still generate tax. Tax is still distributed to everyone. The more technology advances, the greater the contribution of machines to value exchange, and the higher UBI climbs. AI ceases to be humanity's competitor and becomes humanity's benefactor.

### 4.3 Eliminating the Structural Basis for Rent-Seeking

These constraints arise from the base-layer structure of monetary transfer itself — they are not policies but structural impossibilities. The system eliminates the structure that makes rent-seeking inevitable.

No yield on holdings: holding points generates zero return. No lending, no speculation: the 20% tax imposes a cost at every step of any round trip, bleeding speculative strategies until they are unprofitable. Prohibitive cost of share transfer: collaboration pool shares are bound to the real relationship between the investor and the pool; every transfer bears a 20% tax, preventing the formation of any large-scale secondary market for equity.

### 4.4 Why This Can Only Be Done on a Blockchain

The currency described above, automatic taxation on every transfer, automatic distribution of tax proceeds, prohibition of lending, rules frozen beyond anyone's ability to alter — requires a precondition that did not exist before blockchain.

That precondition: the execution of rules cannot depend on any institution that can be bought, pressured, or corrupted.

Without blockchain, implementing such a currency would require trusting some institution to enforce the rules. But any institution entrusted with rule enforcement becomes a control point, and under securitized money, occupying a control point is profitable. Sooner or later, the institution will be captured, will modify the rules, and will become the very extractor it was supposed to prevent. "Entrusting an institution to enforce rules that eliminate rent-seeking" is logically self-contradictory.

Blockchain resolves this contradiction. It allows rules to be written into code that no one can modify, then faithfully executed by a decentralized network of validators — relying not on anyone's integrity but on mathematics and consensus protocols. This is why, throughout human history, no matter how many thinkers proposed similar monetary ideas, none were ever implemented: the institution responsible for execution was always part of the problem.

Blockchain removed this obstacle for the first time. What it can truly do — faithfully execute a set of anti-securitization rules without any trusted institution, is only now becoming visible.

## Chapter 5: Core Mechanism Design

The following rules are intended to be deployed as non-upgradeable smart contracts, immune to modification by any individual, voting mechanism, or external pressure.

### 5.1 Transaction Tax and the Taxation Principle

Whenever any points are transferred into a personal wallet, 20% of the transfer amount is automatically levied as a transaction tax and routed in full to the UBI pool, with two exemptions: UBI distributions and exchange-channel minting. No diversions, no reductions. The 20% figure is a constitutional constant, immutable by design. The system's anti-speculation, anti-rent-seeking, and redistributive efficacy all depend on it.

The taxation principle is a single, extremely simple rule: a transfer is taxable if and only if it moves value from one person to another person. Value that has not reached another person — still circulating within ownerless custody or protocol structures, or returning to the same person, is not a transaction and is not taxed.

This single principle covers all fund flows in the system without supplementary rules. Two special cases merit explicit clarification:

First, entry through the exchange channel. When points are minted in exchange for ETH, the points are new supply created by the reserve pool, not transferred from any person. At the contract level, this is a mint operation, not a transfer, and does not trigger taxation.

Second, UBI distribution. UBI points originate from the system's UBI pool, which has no owner. Once transaction taxes are collected into the UBI pool, their prior ownership attribution is erased. UBI is distributed tax-free, in full, at regular intervals to every active personal wallet.

This means the contract never needs to understand anything about the real world to decide whether to tax. It only needs to check the type of the recipient: if the recipient is a personal wallet, levy 20%; if the recipient is an ownerless custody pool, do not tax. This is a pure, on-chain-verifiable type check. It depends on no inference about transaction intent, and therefore cannot be circumvented by semantic-layer spoofing.

### 5.2 Universal Basic Income

At each settlement cycle, typically weekly, the system distributes the total transaction tax collected during the prior cycle equally to all activated members. Each person receives: prior cycle's total tax revenue ÷ current member count.

UBI issuance is supply-neutral. The total distributed strictly equals the total collected. No net increase in circulating supply, no inflation. UBI is unconditional: no income audit, no nationality filter, no employment requirement. Receipt comes first; contribution follows.

### 5.4 Floating Exchange Rate and the Exchange Channel

The relationship-anchored money world and the external securitized money world are connected by an exchange channel — an ownerless contract holding ETH on one side and minting or burning points on the other. ETH is the sole reserve asset in this channel and the only interface between the points world and the securitized money world. Users who exit receive ETH; converting that ETH into dollars, euros, or any other fiat is their own affair on DEXs or centralized exchanges.

ETH was chosen as the sole reserve for three reasons. First, ETH is Ethereum's native asset, there is no "issuer" who can freeze it. USDC has Circle, USDT has Tether; every compliant stablecoin has an entity behind it that can be compelled by regulators. A single freeze order could paralyze the reserve pool. The reserve pool is the exchange channel's lifeblood; its assets must be unfreezable by anyone. Second, ETH has deep liquidity on every major cryptocurrency exchange globally, one ETH exit covers the entire fiat world. Third, Ethereum is the most decentralized smart contract platform in existence, with hundreds of thousands of independent validators distributed worldwide, consistent with the system's core requirement that rules be tamper-proof.

User-facing prices are displayed in dollars or other fiat units via price oracles: the displayed "1 point = X USD" is a computed presentation value. The on-chain points/ETH rate multiplied by the market ETH/USD price. Actual settlement always occurs between ETH and points.

The channel's functions: mint points when ETH enters, at the current exchange rate; burn points and return ETH when someone exits; and automatically determine entry and exit rates based on the state of the exchange pool and reserve assets.

Tax treatment is straightforward: entry is not taxed, since minting new points is a mint operation, not a transfer, and does not trigger taxation. Exit is not additionally taxed, because points with exit rights have already been taxed at 20% when they arrived in the holder's personal wallet, taxing again would be double taxation.

The exchange rate is not set by order-book matching. It is computed automatically from the ratio of the two assets in the channel via a constant-product formula:

ETH reserve × Points reserve = Constant K

Every exchange must preserve this product. The current rate is simply the ratio of the two reserves: when someone deposits ETH for points, ETH in the channel rises and points fall, making points more expensive. When someone exits, the reverse occurs. Price is a mathematical function of reserve ratios, set by no one.

This formula has a decisive property: the channel can never be fully drained. As the ETH reserve approaches zero, the cost in points to extract it approaches infinity. The exchange window is mathematically permanent, regardless of how many people want to exit, they can always exchange something; only the price moves.

This exchange channel is the sole revolving door between the relationship-anchored money world and the securitized money world. The constant-product formula maintains a mathematically inexhaustible exchange relationship. Points have no off-chain trading venue: any point transfer must traverse the on-chain transfer path and trigger the 20% tax. There is no OTC market that bypasses this gate. The price of points is determined solely by the constant-product formula of this revolving door and cannot be distorted or manipulated by external speculative markets.

The channel's initial state is hardcoded at deployment. The first participant can only deposit at the preset initial rate. Thereafter, the rate is determined entirely by the net flow of market activity.

### 5.5 Payee Redemption Rights

The two sides of the channel are asymmetric, and this asymmetry is the physical embodiment of the entire anti-speculation design. The entry side is fully open: anyone can deposit ETH for points at any time and enter the points economy. The exit side is conditional: only points received as a payee, after the 20% tax has been deducted, are flagged as "redeemable" and can be exchanged back to ETH through the channel.

Points obtained through entry do not carry redemption rights, because the entrant is the payer, not the payee. Only when you have genuinely provided a service to another person within the points economy, and received points as a payee after tax deduction, do those points carry redemption rights. Points distributed as UBI likewise carry no redemption rights: UBI originates from the ownerless UBI pool, not from a person-to-person transaction, and does not meet the definition of payee receipt. UBI can only be spent within the system, and the payee of that spending is the party whose earned points carry redemption rights — value can only exit the system after passing through a real act of service provision.

An entrant hoping to arbitrage, deposit ETH, receive points, exit with ETH, finds themselves stuck: their entry points have no redemption rights. To exit, they must first transfer points to someone else, paying 20% tax and granting the counterparty payee status. This imposes enormous friction; the strategy is only profitable if points appreciate by more than 20% before exit. For providers of goods and services, points earned through payee receipt have already been taxed and carry native redemption rights, they can be exchanged back to ETH at any time.

This amounts to a customs gate between two worlds. It does not inspect motives or ask for identity. It checks a single objective fact: have you left something behind in this economy? It lets anyone in, your ETH strengthens the reserve, benefiting everyone. But to take points out and convert them back to ETH, you must have made a real contribution to this economy.

Upon entry, you added to the reserve. While transacting inside, you paid tax into the UBI pool. When you earned redemption rights through service, you created real value for a real person. The customs gate simply ensures these contributions actually occurred.

### 5.6 Identity Verification

Wallet activation requires one and only one form of verification: World ID's Orb iris scan. World ID is the sole identity verification method, because identity verification is the foundation on which UBI depends.

The activation flow is simple. A user completes an Orb iris scan via the World App, generating a zero-knowledge proof. The proof is submitted to the contract, which verifies its validity and checks that the proof's nullifier has never been used before — preventing the same person from registering two wallets. On verification, the wallet is activated and begins receiving UBI. Eligibility derives from the iris, from the simple fact of being a human being.

World ID was selected because it is currently the only identity verification protocol that is biologically unforgeable, independent of government documents, free of charge, privacy-preserving through zero-knowledge proofs, and fully open-source in both hardware and software specifications, independent of any proprietary technical barrier.

Government documents can be forged. Facial recognition can be defeated by deepfakes. Phone number verification can be bulk-acquired. Only iris patterns are, under current technology, virtually impossible to spoof. If a weaker verification method were allowed as a fallback, attackers would inevitably choose the weakest option, and the system's overall security would equal the security of its weakest method.

A second rule is the periodic re-verification mechanism. Each activated personal wallet records a last-verified timestamp. The system requires at least one World ID re-verification per year to refresh this timestamp. Wallets that exceed the one-year grace period without re-verification enter dormancy — they are excluded from the UBI denominator and stop receiving UBI. Balances in dormant wallets are preserved in place; upon re-verification, UBI calculation and distribution resume.

This ensures the UBI denominator always reflects the current count of genuinely alive and participating individuals, rather than an ever-growing historical accumulation inflated by dead and abandoned wallets. A deceased person's wallet continues receiving UBI for at most one more year before being cut off, limiting dilution of the UBI pool to an acceptable margin.

Inheritance is handled through a fully optional, user-controlled mechanism. A user can set beneficiaries and a trigger duration, specifying how long without re-verification before being presumed deceased, using iris verification. The trigger duration is set by the user themselves. If not configured, the balance remains permanently frozen in the on-chain wallet after death. Once the trigger duration elapses without re-verification, anyone can initiate inheritance settlement, distributing the balance to designated beneficiaries at the preset ratios; each receipt is taxed at the standard 20%.

Throughout the trigger window, the owner can reset the timer at any time by completing a single iris re-verification, overturning the presumption of death. The window length is set by the user at configuration time, giving someone who merely lost their private key or was away for an extended period ample opportunity to recover. Beneficiary designation is set personally by the owner; modification requires iris verification.

### 5.7 Challenge-Response Mechanism

The identity verification mechanism incidentally solves the problem of lost private keys. In the broader crypto world, losing a wallet's private key means the wallet is effectively lost and the funds with it. In the relationship-anchored money system, identity is anchored to the iris rather than to a losable private key — effectively giving every person a biometric wallet recovery mechanism.

A technical mechanism requires explanation: World ID generates a deterministic nullifier for the same person on the same application. How do we ensure that a re-verification uses a freshly generated proof from a live session, rather than replaying a proof generated a year ago, or even one from a deceased person?

The answer is a challenge-response mechanism. World ID's zero-knowledge proof binds not only the nullifier but also an arbitrary signal specified by the application. On each re-verification request, the system generates a random challenge with a short expiration window, typically one hour. The user must bring this challenge to an Orb, complete a live scan, and the Orb binds the challenge into the newly generated ZK proof as the signal.


## Chapter 6: Collaboration Pools: A Unified Mechanism for Organization

The preceding chapters reshaped money itself. From this chapter forward, we examine what it reshapes above the monetary layer: a comprehensive revolution in how people organize to collaborate.

In this system, corporations, partnerships, supply chains, cooperatives, mutual aid societies, industry associations, charities, village public works, and a few friends pooling resources for a weekend project, every arrangement where people come together, collaborate, and agree on how funds or revenue are distributed — is reduced to a single primitive: the collaboration pool. Whether for-profit or not-for-profit, permanent or ad hoc, three people or thirty thousand, they share the same underlying structure. The only differences are parameters and nesting depth. A "company" is just one of many organizational forms; the collaboration pool unifies them all.

This chapter explains the collaboration pool mechanism itself: what it is, how it is created, how money flows through it, how rules are established and changed, how capital participates, and how it scales technically to arbitrary size. The next chapter examines what it means: how it dismantles the debt-and-credit-centered organizational model of the old world, and just how deep that transformation goes.

### 6.1 What a Collaboration Pool Is: Permissionless, Ownerless, Instant

Creating a collaboration pool has no barrier to entry and requires no one's approval. Any individual or group can create one at any time. A sole proprietor can hold 100% share; partners can split by negotiated ratios.

Creation is simply invoking publicly available code, open equally to everyone. The code's only function is to ensure that the resulting pool conforms to the structural rules described below — rules designed to close loopholes for tax evasion and misappropriation, applied uniformly to all pools.

A collaboration pool has no independent legal personality, no will that overrides its participants, and does not own the funds inside it. It is simply a vessel for consensus, an arrangement, written in a contract, agreed to by all participants, and exitable by any participant at any time, about how to collaborate and distribute revenue.

### 6.2 Ownerless: No Discretionary Exit for Funds

Collaboration pools are ownerless. No individual has ownership of or discretionary control over the funds inside.

Funds exit the pool through exactly two channels: first, flowing to another collaboration pool in accordance with a supply-chain relationship hardcoded in the contract. This remains custodial flow and is not taxed; second, accruing to a specific person's accumulated share according to the distribution rules hardcoded in the contract. This share flows one-way to their personal wallet. When money reaches a person, it is taxed at 20%. Both the destinations and the proportions of these two exits are constants set at pool creation.

The meaning of "ownerless" is precisely this: fund exits are hardcoded, and no one can initiate an ad hoc discretionary transfer. To pay a new recipient, the only path is to modify the pool's rules, and revenue accrued under the old rules is unaffected by any new rule.

### 6.3 Governance: Shareholders and Payees

A collaboration pool maintains two sets of information: a shareholder registry and a distribution rule set. The shareholder registry records who holds governance rights, defined by whether an address holds equity share in the pool — regardless of whether that address is a personal wallet or another collaboration pool.

An investment vehicle pool that contributed capital and an individual who contributed capital have identical governance standing: both are shareholders. The distribution rules record which addresses receive funds and at what proportions. These addresses include both shareholders and non-shareholder payees in the upstream or downstream supply chain.

Shareholders hold governance rights: any change to the distribution rules requires unanimous consent of all shareholders. Non-shareholder payees appear in the distribution rules but are not on the shareholder registry and have no voting power. Their relationship to the pool is commercial, not a governance relationship.

This means shareholders can adjust a supplier's share, replace a supplier, or terminate a service team's engagement by reaching agreement among themselves alone, without the supplier's consent. A party whose terms are adjusted can terminate the commercial relationship at any time and seek other clients.

### 6.4 Rule Changes and Accounting Cycles

There is no such thing as "undistributed profit" in a pool. The instant revenue enters a pool, it is immediately attributed to each member according to the current distribution rules. If you hold 30%, every 100 points that arrives means 30 are logically yours, just not yet transferred to your personal wallet. Every unit of currency inside the pool at any moment has a definite owner. No funds are ever in an "ownership undetermined" state.

A distribution rule defines an accounting cycle. Changing the current distribution rules, whether adding or removing a payer or payee or adjusting proportions, ends the current cycle and begins a new one. Ending the current cycle is simple: the amounts logically attributed to each person during the cycle are transferred to their personal wallets, triggering the 20% tax. This step merely settles an already-determined attribution on-chain. After the new rules take effect, subsequent revenue is attributed under the new proportions.

For shareholder disagreements, day-to-day operations run on default consent: distribution ratios are agreed upon unanimously by all shareholders at pool creation and written into the contract. Thereafter, distributions under the established rules execute automatically without requiring repeated signatures. Any shareholder who believes an arrangement harms them can submit a dissent to block a rule change.

Deadlocks are resolved through negotiation: either reach a new consensus, or the other shareholders offer the dissenter a satisfactory price to buy out their share. No one can eliminate a shareholder's share through restructuring — that would amount to confiscation of their investment. Exit for a non-shareholder service provider means ceasing collaboration. Exit for a shareholder means transferring their share to someone else.

The mechanism's real significance: no one can retroactively seize what someone else has already earned by changing the rules. Prior-cycle revenue is attributed under prior-cycle rules. Rule changes affect only the future. Whether a person holds 90% or 1% of equity, they are equally bound by the rules, equally protected by the "settle old accounts before new rules take effect" guarantee, and equally empowered to exit as a response to arrangements they refuse to accept. Before the rules, 90% and 1% are equal.

### 6.5 Revenue and Capital: Non-Fungible Tracks

It is worth spelling out the fund relationships inside a pool clearly, because they explain why this system barely needs accounting.

At any moment, a pool holds exactly two things: capital contributed by members, now absorbed into operations and recorded under each person's name as the basis for their share; and revenue that has flowed in and been instantly attributed to each member by share, taxed upon withdrawal to personal accounts.

Once contributed, capital is absorbed into operations and becomes part of the pool's operating resources. It is spent on procurement and outgoing payments according to the pool's rules. Investor returns are realized through ongoing profit distributions. Exit is achieved by transferring shares to someone else. If a member wants to reinvest distributed income back into the pool as new capital, the money must first land in their personal account, where it is taxed, then be redeposited from their personal account as a new capital contribution. The intervening tax cannot be avoided: it is the cost of doing business.

Revenue and capital thus run on two non-fungible tracks. Profit-and-loss netting exists in old-world accounting because the corporation is treated as a continuous, unified entity whose inflows and outflows can offset each other over time. Here, the pool is an ownerless conduit that money flows through — capital enters from one end and is consumed; revenue enters from the other end and is distributed. Each track runs independently, tracked separately by the contract, with no interface allowing anyone to mix them. If a venture loses money, the "loss" is simply reflected in the fact that contributed capital did not produce commensurate revenue: a reality already fully captured in the remaining numbers.

This makes taxation extremely simple. No year-end reconciliation, no judgment calls on whether a given flow is revenue or return of capital, no depreciation or amortization concepts. One rule: when money reaches your personal account, 20% is levied. The entire tax logic is clean enough to make accountants obsolete.

### 6.6 Infinite Nesting: One Set of Rules from Workshop to Multinational Network

Collaboration pools can nest infinitely, and this is precisely what allows them to support organizations of arbitrary scale and complexity. A small workshop is a single collaboration pool. A multi-hundred-person enterprise with multiple departments is a parent pool with departmental sub-pools nested beneath, which may in turn contain further sub-pools. A supply chain spanning multiple independent enterprises is each enterprise's pool nested into a larger supply-chain pool. A national charity is local chapter pools nested into a parent pool, with donations flowing between pools and ultimately reaching specific beneficiaries or operators.

Whether for-profit or not-for-profit, regardless of structural complexity or nesting depth, every layer obeys the same rules: ownerless, equity-based governance, default consent with dissent veto, free exit, tax-free inter-pool flow, taxation only upon reaching personal wallets. A three-person bakery and a thirty-thousand-person manufacturing network, a multinational charity, run on identical rules. Only nesting depth differs. Complexity lives in the nesting structure; the rules remain the same throughout, requiring no new constructs as scale or purpose changes.

Nesting is a purely structural capability. The protocol provides one thing: the ability for one pool to designate another pool as upstream and route revenue to it at a hardcoded ratio. How to nest, how many layers deep, who combines with whom — all of this is left entirely to users to organize according to their actual needs. The next chapter shows how this simple nesting capability allows collaboration pools to grow into every conceivable organizational form.

### 6.7 Technical Implementation for Large-Scale Collaboration

For nesting to support tens of thousands of collaborators without collapsing under computational load, distribution computation must be independent of participant count.

The actual accounting uses a "cumulative reward per share" pattern: each time new revenue enters a pool, only a single global variable, cumulative earnings per share, is updated.

When a participant withdraws, they compute their entitled amount as the difference between the current global cumulative value and their own snapshot at last withdrawal, multiplied by their share. This makes the on-chain computational cost per incoming revenue unit fixed, regardless of whether the pool has ten or a hundred thousand participants. This pattern has been validated in existing DeFi protocols, supporting dividend distribution across hundreds of thousands of addresses.

Participants withdraw on their own schedule, daily, weekly, monthly. Only upon withdrawal does a real on-chain transfer occur, triggering that single tax event. Worth clarifying: where does the money actually sit, and when exactly is tax levied? When revenue enters a pool, a real transfer occurs: money moves from the consumer into the pool contract's address. But "how much of this money belongs to which member" is purely an accounting entry in the pool's ledger.

A member's earned-but-unwithdrawn revenue does not sit in their personal wallet, it sits, alongside other members' shares, in the pool contract's address, with the ledger tracking their entitlement. Only at the moment of withdrawal does their share move from the pool contract to their personal wallet, and this is the first time this money has reached any personal wallet, so tax is levied at precisely this moment.

Aggregating all of a person's earned-but-unwithdrawn revenue across every pool they participate in yields their "pre-tax accrual balance", funds already attributed to them, sitting in pre-tax state, waiting for them to withdraw at their convenience. This is purely a view-layer aggregation; the underlying money remains distributed across individual pool contracts, so the aggregation costs nothing. On withdrawal, a person can pull from a single pool or batch-withdraw from all pools in a single operation, each pool transferring independently and each levying its tax. Timing is the person's own gas-cost optimization: small, frequent earnings accumulate; needed cash is available instantly. The experience feels like receiving a paycheck, but the underlying mechanism is per-contribution accrual with no one deciding "how much to pay them."

In this system, every unit of points at any moment exists in exactly one of two states: attributed to a person but still in pre-tax accrual, unwithdrawn, untaxed, physically sitting in the pool contract; or in a person's personal wallet, taxed, freely spendable, transferable, or redeemable.

### 6.8 Arbitration: The Interface with the Real World

The monetary layer itself needs no adjudication mechanism, on-chain rules are transparent and deterministic, leaving no room for dispute. What truly requires adjudication is a different matter: whether the real-world event corresponding to a clear on-chain transaction actually occurred, and whether it met the agreed standard. Was the roof actually repaired? What is the quality of the delivered goods? Is a mutual aid claim legitimate? These are disputes about reality, beyond the chain's visibility, beyond code's jurisdiction.

The system addresses this through an optional arbitration layer attached to individual transactions. It is not mandatory: instant-settlement transactions or those between mutually trusting parties do not need it. Only transactions involving "payment first, real-world fulfillment later, with potential for dispute" opt into it.

The mechanism: for transactions requiring protection, funds are deposited into an escrow pool. At creation, both parties jointly specify three things: who the arbitrator is, the escrow duration, and a pre-agreed fixed arbitration fee. The arbitrator can be a trusted acquaintance, a local government court system, or a professional arbitration body — chosen by the parties according to the nature and size of the transaction. The protocol does not prescribe who the arbitrator must be; it merely provides the interface for both parties to jointly designate an arbitrator address. Once set, these three parameters cannot be unilaterally changed.

If no dispute is raised during the escrow period, funds are automatically released to the seller at expiration, final and irreversible. If the buyer believes fulfillment was deficient, they can trigger a dispute, posting a deposit to deter frivolous claims; the losing party's deposit goes to the arbitrator as compensation for their verification work. The arbitrator investigates the real-world situation, then issues an on-chain ruling specifying a split ratio. The funds are distributed between buyer and seller only, anywhere from full refund to full release, and the arbitrator cannot redirect any portion to third parties including themselves. Arbitration is final in one round; there is no on-chain appeal. Justice depends on the prudence with which parties select their arbitrator upfront.

In practice, for most transactions requiring arbitration protection, both parties will naturally choose a credible real-world institution, whether a local court, police force, or professional arbitration body, because these have real investigative capacity and institutional trust. Arbitration fees thus become a natural, service-based revenue stream for these institutions, including governments.

### 6.9 Mutual Aid: Implementing the Insurance Function

A mutual aid pool is a variant of the collaboration pool designed for risk-sharing. Its structure is simple: a group of people each deposit points into an ownerless pool. Deposits are tax-free. Funds in the pool are pooled and no longer distinguished by contributor. When a member experiences a pre-agreed risk event, the pool pays out to their personal account, with the payout taxed at 20% on receipt.

This mechanism is essentially a voluntary, conditionally triggered redistribution pool. It differs from the UBI pool in three ways: participation is voluntary, each person decides whether to join, how much to contribute, and at what frequency; triggering is conditional. Only pre-agreed risk events trigger payouts, while UBI distributes unconditionally on a regular basis; and management can be delegated. The pool can designate a professional claims management organization to handle claims adjudication.

The claims management organization plays a role similar to the arbitrator described in 6.8, but on a standing basis. From the funds members regularly deposit, a "management fee share" can be allocated at creation, automatically distributed to the management organization's collaboration pool by a preset ratio. This gives the management organization a stable revenue stream tied to the mutual aid pool's size. Its responsibilities are to adjudicate whether a claimed event is legitimate and to handle day-to-day operations; but it does not hold premium funds. Funds always remain in the ownerless mutual aid pool, beyond the management organization's reach.

UBI already covers the most basic income protection function. Mutual aid pools handle the risks UBI cannot: low-frequency, high-severity events like major illness, natural disasters, and accidents. Participants choose which mutual aid pools to join and how much to contribute based on their own risk assessment, entirely a personal decision, with no system mandate.

This mechanism addresses a structural problem in the securitized money world's insurance industry. Traditional insurers hold large pools of premium capital and invest it for profit, investment returns often exceed claims payouts, turning insurance into a financial business dressed up as risk-sharing. In low-rate environments, this model reaches its logical endpoint: to maintain returns, insurers are forced to push premiums into increasingly risky assets, from private credit to structured products to related-party lending. Capital meant to provide people with safety is invested in complex financial products bearing no relation to those people's actual risks. The entire insurance industry, under this pressure, is morphing into another form of investment bank — managing the pensions and life coverage of hundreds of millions.

Mutual aid pools plus claims management organizations constitute the next-generation insurance company. Management organizations compete on service quality and charge transparent management fees. Premium capital stays in the ownerless mutual aid pool awaiting real claims, never diverted into high-risk investments. Insurance returns to its original meaning: a group of people pool their money, and whoever has trouble draws from the pool.

## Chapter 7: The Transformation of Economic Organization

The preceding chapters described the protocol's core rules and the collaboration pool as a unified organizational primitive. This chapter shows how these rules reshape every form of concrete economic activity. Its central theme is a fundamental shift in foundations: from a debt-centered economy to an equity-centered economy.

### 7.1 From Debt to Equity: Replacing the Foundation of the Economy

Economic activity under securitized money is built, from the ground up, on debt. A company accumulates debt obligations from its first day of existence: bank loans provide startup capital, suppliers extend credit for raw materials, leases commit to future payments, employee wages are fixed monthly promises that must be honored, and customer prepayments are liabilities awaiting fulfillment. The common feature of these relationships is rigidity: regardless of how the venture performs, the amounts must be paid when due, or the entity defaults. The entire company is a machine woven from rigid commitments of various sizes, and "management" is largely the art of navigating the timing gaps and cash-flow shortfalls between them.

This rigidity penetrates every capillary of economic activity. Consumer credit-card spending is debt. A mortgage is a thirty-year rigid commitment. A student loan is an eighteen-year-old overdrawing future income. Insurance premiums create a conditional repayment obligation. Banks take in deposits, short-term repayment obligations, and convert them into long-term loans, profiting from the maturity mismatch. The entire financial system is debt stacked on debt, each layer depending on the layer below paying on time.

Within this structure, the "corporation" as an independent legal entity exists fundamentally to serve as a debtor. It needs a legal personality because creditors need an entity to pursue claims against. It needs a standalone balance sheet because creditors need to assess repayment capacity. It needs complex accounting standards because different types of debt have different priorities and maturities. It needs bankruptcy law because when repayment capacity is exhausted, there must be rules for distributing limited residual value among competing creditors. Corporate law, accounting standards, bankruptcy law, credit ratings, auditing. This entire institutional infrastructure exists, at root, to manage the consequences of debt relationships.

In the relationship-anchored money system, lending is structurally nonexistent, and the entire debt edifice loses its foundation. In its place stands an economy built entirely on equity relationships. Investors contribute points in exchange for a percentage share of a collaboration pool, and receive a proportional share of the pool's real revenue. Supplier relationships are supply-chain links, with fees collected at hardcoded ratios. No credit extensions. Members receive profit distributions by share. No fixed wage commitments. No one holds a rigid "pay me this amount by this date" claim against any pool.

The fundamental difference between equity and debt: debt demands a fixed, reality-detached promise — you must honor this number regardless of whether things go well or badly. Equity accepts uncertainty, returns track real outcomes, sharing upside and absorbing downside. Debt's rigidity looks safe and reliable in good times; in bad times it becomes a lethal contagion chain, one default cascades to the next creditor, who defaults on their creditor, chain-reaction collapse. Equity's flexibility smoothly reflects reality in both good and bad times, volatility is absorbed, not transmitted. This is the fundamental shift from debt to equity foundations. Every specific change described in the sections that follow , from organizational form to tax structure to the disappearance of bankruptcy, is a corollary of this shift.

### 7.2 The Fundamental Transformation of Labor Relations

Wages in the securitized money world are fundamentally a debt claim: employers promise a fixed monthly amount regardless of actual business performance. This fixed commitment makes the entire labor market fragile. During downturns, companies cannot adjust this rigid expense; their only option is layoffs, unilateral default on the wage commitment. Laid-off workers' income drops to zero overnight, their spending capacity vanishes, and their absence as consumers causes more companies' revenue to decline, leading to more layoffs. This is the concrete manifestation of rigid debt contagion in the labor market: one node's default cascades down the chain until the entire labor market enters a recessionary spiral.

In this system, labor compensation still exists, but its nature shifts from a fixed employer promise, a "wage debt", to revenue distribution within a supply chain. Every person participates in economic activity through their own pool or as a member of other pools, with income tied to real profit distributions that directly reflect actual output.

A baker is not an employee of a bakery chain collecting a monthly salary. They are a member of the bakery's collaboration pool, receiving a share of the pool's real weekly profit. Or they operate as an individual pool with a service supply relationship to the bakery, receiving their negotiated share of labor costs. Or they form a team with two partners, working together at the bakery with stronger collective bargaining power, distributing compensation internally by their own agreed-upon terms.

This transformation is achieved through pool nesting. The outermost layer is a decision pool whose members are a few partners or core decision-makers plus investors; rule changes within this pool require only these few people to agree. Each employee team, department, and supplier is an independent upstream pool, connected to the decision pool by a hardcoded supply-chain relationship. The engineering team's pool distributes internally to each engineer's personal wallet according to its own rules. The decision layer can adjust a team's allocation or swap suppliers by reaching agreement within the decision pool alone. No company-wide signature collection required. The team pool's internal distribution is under the team's own control, not the decision pool's.

The profound shift: the relationship between "boss" and "employee" becomes a relationship between "buyer" and "supplier". A relationship between equals. An engineering team is no longer hired, managed, and unilaterally assigned a salary. It is an independent service provider. A group of engineers with their own internal distribution rules, constituting an independent pool. If the decision layer thinks the team is too expensive, they can switch providers. If the team thinks the price is too low, they can leave as a unit and serve another decision pool. The departed team continues to exist as an intact pool, immediately available to onboard new clients. The old world's most effective tool against workers, divide and conquer through information asymmetry, is impossible here, because distribution rules are on-chain and public; everyone's share is visible to all.

This structure has a natural side effect: workers organically form something resembling unions. A team pool is a self-organized collective of workers controlling their own internal income distribution. They negotiate compensation collectively with the decision pool; internal allocation is their own affair. This is not a right to be fought for or an organizational form requiring institutional protection. It is simply the natural result of collaboration pool nesting.

Under this structure, the distinction between "employment" and "entrepreneurship" fundamentally dissolves. Joining an existing team pool to provide services, and forming a new team pool with partners to serve clients, are mechanically identical, seamlessly interchangeable. In the securitized money world, unemployment is terrifying because it means the rupture of a fixed promise — an abrupt transition from "stable income" to "zero income." Here there is no such rupture: a team pool that loses one client can immediately pursue another, or members can individually join other team pools. Workers are the agents of organization, bargaining, and distribution. Not factors waiting to be selected.

### 7.3 Every Collaboration Pool Is an Investment Target

Because every collaboration pool in the system is inherently an equity structure — from a five-person engineering team to a corner bakery to a multinational supply-chain project. The universe of investable targets becomes extraordinarily broad. Every pool's complete operating data is on-chain and publicly verifiable, enabling investors to make decisions on a foundation of total transparency.

In the securitized money world, only enterprises that have undergone complex legal and financial packaging, listing approval, and regulatory compliance can accept public investment. Here, any operating pool can accept investment, with the only barrier being mutual willingness to negotiate. Investment shifts from a specialized activity requiring extensive intermediary and institutional infrastructure to an everyday act accessible to anyone.

Investment here is an irreversible phase transition: a static lump of capital enters a pool, is absorbed into real operations, and transforms into wages, raw materials, service fees, flowing to real people. It no longer exists in any static form. Returns flow back as ongoing cash flows. A finite principal is converted through real economic activity into a potentially unlimited income stream. The investor's role is "the person who makes the economy flow": they inject idle stock into real production and service, and in return draw a continuous, proportional share of the resulting flow.

This means that the more capital a person holds, the more rational it becomes to deploy it into high-quality teams, converting it into cash flow and fueling the economic cycle. Holding cash yields nothing, no interest, no appreciation, just a number sitting idle. Capital transforms from a form of power that profits merely from being held into a tool that has meaning only in motion. Its entire function is to expand the network of value exchange relationships, and the moment it stops moving, it is nothing. What truly defines a person's economic position is the aggregate cash flow from their shares across various pools. Not the static balance in their account.

Investors see raw on-chain cash flow: every revenue entry, every expense, every distribution, real-time, complete, unprocessed by any intermediary. AI analysis tools can compute profit trends, payback periods, upstream cost structures, and peer pool comparisons in seconds. Depreciation, amortization, accrual accounting, deferred revenue, goodwill. These traditional accounting concepts serve no purpose here, because what investors read is reality itself, with no water to squeeze out.

A pool seeking investment naturally showcases its on-chain data: the past six months of real net cash flow, growth trends, upstream cost structure, member count changes, then presents an investment proposal: "Invest 10,000 points for 5% of future profits; at current profit levels, estimated 14-month payback." The fundamental difference from the securitized money world's investment pitch: every number here is a verifiable on-chain historical fact. Anyone can independently verify. AI tools can cross-reference the complete transaction flows of upstream and downstream pools. Every wallet address corresponds to a real, iris-verified person. Fraud here is extremely expensive, because it requires simultaneously fabricating transaction records across an entire supply chain of pools belonging to different people.

Investment vehicles can also exist. Several investors form an investment pool, each contributing capital and sharing returns by ratio. The investment pool deploys capital to other collaboration pools in exchange for shares. When invested pools generate profit, returns flow to the investment pool as inter-pool transfers, tax-free. The investment pool then distributes to each investor's personal account, triggering the 20% tax at that point. The real value an investment institution provides here is due diligence and investment judgment, and charging a transparent management fee is entirely legitimate. The difference from investment firms in the securitized money world: all holdings are fully on-chain transparent, fee structures are written into pool rules visible to everyone, leverage is impossible, and fund flows cannot be manipulated behind closed doors.

### 7.4 Payback Period, Dilution, and Share Transfer

The core metric for investors shifts from "at what price can I sell to the next person" to "how long until payback." Invest 10,000 points. At the current profit distribution rate, how long until cumulative dividends received exceed 10,000? After payback, every dividend is pure profit, already real, already irreversible, already in the personal account. It will not evaporate. It has nothing to do with market sentiment.

Investment transforms from a bet on future stock price into a direct purchase of real cash flow. The psychology after payback is also entirely different: in the securitized money world, stock investors with unrealized gains are anxious about "should I sell", because until they sell, it is not realized, and the stock price could drop back at any moment. Here, that anxiety does not exist. Profits have arrived one by one, real and irreversible, in the personal account.

Share ratios are determined by bilateral negotiation. Founders know their pool's real operating data; so does the prospective investor, it is all on-chain. Both sides negotiate on the basis of identical, complete information: "you invest 10,000 for 5% of future profits." The same 10,000 invested at the venture's inception , when the pool had no revenue and success was uncertain, might negotiate 20% or more. Later-stage investment means lower risk, earning a smaller share. This is the same logic as venture capital in the securitized money world , seed rounds are cheap, later rounds are expensive, except that the valuation basis here is a fully auditable on-chain history of real cash flow. Both parties see the same data. There is no information asymmetry to exploit.

Introducing a new investor means changing the existing share distribution ratios. A rule change requiring unanimous shareholder consent, just like changing upstream payment flows or adjusting member ratios. In the securitized money world, unanimous consent is unthinkable because a public company may have millions of shareholders, forcing proxy decision-making through a board of directors — which creates the structural oppression of minority shareholders by large shareholders and management.

In the relationship-anchored money system, this threshold is entirely workable because the power to raise capital is distributed to the smallest value-creating unit. A five-person engineering team's pool may have only one or two investors. At that scale, a few people sit down, reach agreement, and write it into the contract. This also makes predatory capital expansion structurally impossible: expansion must be built on genuine consensus among all existing participants.

Share transfer can technically occur: A and B agree on a price off-chain. B pays A, triggering the 20% transaction tax on receipt. A replaces their endpoint receiving address with B's address. This operation does not change distribution ratios, does not add a new address, does not affect any other member's share. It merely redirects A's income stream from A to B. Since it affects only A's portion, it does not require other members' consent.

The 20% tax here follows the same logic as every other transaction tax in the system: imposing weight on transfer. In the securitized money world, equity can be transferred at zero cost, zero friction, in milliseconds, precisely because transfer is weightless, it can be packaged into a security, traded as a chip on secondary markets, with prices driven by expectations and sentiment far from real value.

The 20% tax turns every share transfer into a decision requiring serious deliberation: is this transaction genuinely worth both parties bearing this cost? This pushes the vast majority of speculative, emotional, and value-irrelevant transfers into the "not worth it" zone. Large-scale secondary markets require high frequency, low friction, and standardization. The 20% tax directly negates all three conditions. Shares can change hands in small circles based on real relationships and considered judgment, but large-scale stock markets naturally vanish.

If a majority of shareholders agree to bring in a new investor but one existing investor objects to dilution, the deadlock is resolved through price: the others offer the objector a satisfactory price for their share. The objector receives payment, taxed at 20%, exits, and the remaining shareholders unanimously admit the new investor. No one is coerced. The objector sold voluntarily, the remaining shareholders diluted voluntarily. The high bar of "unanimous consent" does not produce deadlocks in practice: you either accept dilution, or accept a buyout price that satisfies you.

### 7.5 The Impossibility of Re-Securitization

In the securitized money world, both debt and equity are subjected to the same operation: packaging, transfer, and secondary market trading.

A loan, a share — each is originally a real economic relationship between people. But the relationship is reduced to a number, a face value or a valuation, and numbers can be transferred independently, detached from the relationship. Each transfer moves the number holder one step further from the real relationship underneath, until no one knows whether there is still a real relationship supporting the number. This was the core mechanism of the subprime crisis: mortgages that could never be repaid were packaged layer upon layer into seemingly safe products and sold to global investors. When underlying borrowers began defaulting en masse, the layered products collapsed.

Equity follows the same pattern. Stocks originally represented a share of a venture's real future cash flow. But the modern stock market stacked a massive secondary trading market on top of this real equity, turning stocks into chips to be flipped repeatedly, with prices driven by expectations rather than fundamentals. People buy stocks not primarily for dividends but to bet that the price will rise and they can sell at a higher price to the next person. Value has been re-securitized.

This system eliminates re-securitization at the root. Debt does not exist, so there is nothing to package. Collaboration pool shares are bound to a real income distribution right; transfer merely replaces the endpoint address; every transfer bears a 20% tax — making large-scale secondary markets unviable at this cost. Numbers are still used for accounting, but numbers are structurally anchored to the real relationship they record and cannot circulate independently detached from that relationship.

Investment is thus reduced to its only honest form: everything is value investing, because there is nothing to invest in except real value.

### 7.6 The Natural Evolution of Organizational Form

When lending does not exist, wages disappear, every individual becomes an independent node in the supply chain, and investment and entrepreneurship become everyday activities. The organizational form of the entire economy undergoes a fundamental, natural transformation.

Giant corporations lose their economic rationale. In the securitized money world, controlling fund flows is itself profitable, so economic organizations naturally tend toward intermediation. The more money passes through your hands, the greater your power. The entire concept of a "company" as an independent legal entity holding its own funds and wielding authority over its members has its root here.

In this system, routing funds through an intermediate node produces no revenue; eliminating one intermediate node saves one layer of tax. A highly influential, highly profitable "company" in this system may be nothing more than a small decision pool of a few people, interfacing with a variety of independent team pools and supplier pools upstream and downstream. The "company" shrinks to a coordination node; real value creation is distributed across the entire supply-chain network.

An engineering team pool can independently publish a funding announcement, display its on-chain operating data, and attract independent investors — funding entirely unrelated to the top-level decision pool of the "large project" the team serves. Risk is naturally isolated: if a team pool fails, it affects only that pool's investors and members, with zero impact on other teams' investors within the same project, because inter-pool relationships are supply-chain relationships, not equity subordination.

Under this structure, "entrepreneurship," "investment," and "employment," three categories strictly separated in the securitized money world, merge. Every person is simultaneously a service provider, an investor, and a consumer. An engineer might provide services in their team pool, hold investment shares in three other pools, and use dividends to purchase daily services. Entrepreneurial income and investment dividends become everyone's actual income source, not wages. Account balances become a temporary waystation — money distributed from a pool to a personal account is either spent on consumption or invested in another pool for shares. Leaving it idle in the account has no benefit whatsoever. Wealth shifts from "how much you hold" to "how many real economic activities you participate in and how much real value those activities produce."

In this radically decentralized economic structure, "AI replacing human jobs" becomes an entirely obsolete proposition. Every word in that proposition is embedded in the securitized money framework: "job" means selling labor time for a wage claim; "replacing" means capital choosing between two inputs on a cost basis; "human" is an input in the same category as a machine, competing against it.

In this system, people are the economy's purpose. UBI guarantees every person's baseline economic rights, independent of "usefulness." AI is a productivity multiplier for individuals: a three-person team that uses AI to quintuple output sees each member's dividends increase fivefold. No one is laid off, because there are no employees. Only independent service providers. All of AI's productivity gains stay with the people who use it, because they are the pool's owners.

The system inherently incentivizes AI adoption: in the securitized money world, the incentive for AI adoption is cutting headcount to reduce costs. Here there is no headcount to cut. AI-driven productivity gains mean higher pool profits, which mean higher dividends for all members and investors, and more transaction volume means higher UBI.

### 7.7 Tax Structure: Why Corporate Tax Does Not Exist

The concept of a corporation as an independent taxpayer presupposes that it is an independent debtor — an entity with its own balance sheet and the capacity to independently bear legal obligations. Under securitized money, this presupposition holds: the corporation has borrowed, owes debts, and signed contracts. It needs an independent legal personality to bear these obligations, and taxing this legal personality is a natural extension of the arrangement.

In the relationship-anchored money system, this presupposition no longer exists. Pools have no legal personality, cannot borrow, owe nothing to anyone, and have no legal obligations requiring independent assumption. A pool is merely a transparent conduit through which money flows. Inter-pool transfers are entirely tax-free. "Taxing a corporation" has no taxable entity here.

Tax occurs only when value reaches a real person's personal wallet: the 20% transaction tax is automatically deducted into the UBI pool. Government taxation can be levied as a percentage of each person's annual personal wallet inflows, collected through a government-operated collaboration pool, as described in Chapter 8. The entire tax burden is concentrated at a single point, personal receipt, and levied once.

In the securitized money world, a corporation is a legal fiction. It does not benefit from policing, does not walk on roads, does not get sick and need healthcare. The real beneficiaries of public services are real people. Taxing a fictional entity amounts to intercepting value before it reaches the real beneficiaries.

This creates the entire arbitrage space for offshore tax avoidance: park profits inside the fictional entity, register the fictional entity in the lowest-tax jurisdiction, and ensure value never formally "reaches" individuals. The entire offshore financial industry exists because of the premise that "a corporation is an independent taxpayer." In the points system, pools are not taxpayers, have no concept of jurisdictional registration on-chain, and the rules in the code apply equally to everyone. The arbitrage structure simply does not exist.

### 7.8 The Disappearance of Bankruptcy

Push the same logic to its endpoint, and the concept of "bankruptcy" loses its premise.

Bankruptcy means insolvency: an entity's accumulated liabilities exceed its capacity to repay, requiring a legal process to allocate limited residual value among competing creditors. This process is itself extremely expensive, time-consuming, and produces cascading harm: one company's bankruptcy means all its creditors absorb losses; those creditors may owe debts to others, so defaults propagate down the debt chain. A sufficiently large bankruptcy can trigger chain reactions across an entire industry or economy. Lehman Brothers' 2008 collapse triggering the global financial crisis was the ultimate demonstration of this contagion chain.

In the collaboration pool structure, pools cannot borrow, suppliers do not extend credit, members do not receive fixed wages, and no one holds a rigid "pay me this amount by this date" claim against any pool. Insolvency is logically impossible, because there is no "debt" to be insolvent against.

A pool that performs poorly reaches a quiet end: revenue declines, member cash flows shrink, service providers decide the income isn't worth their time and leave to serve other pools, investors decide returns are too low and stop contributing, operating funds are depleted, and the pool naturally ceases to function. No legal proceedings, no liquidation, no creditors queuing for residual value, no cascading defaults. Investors lose the capital they already deployed, but they knew this risk when they invested. The pool's complete operating data has been on-chain since day one. Service providers simply find another client. No one is "owed" anything.

This also means the systemic risk triggered by corporate bankruptcy is eliminated at the root. A large pool ceasing operations affects only its direct members and investors; it does not propagate outward through debt chains, because there are no debt chains. The domino effect of the securitized money world — "a large company fails, dragging down its supply chain, the banks on the supply chain follow, and the banks' depositors suffer", has no physical basis here. Economic fluctuations will still occur, but fluctuations are absorbed by equity's flexibility within each pool rather than amplified by debt's rigidity along cascading chains.

### 7.9 Platforms and Intermediaries

In the securitized money world, the platform business model is built on routing fund flows: consumer payments land first in the platform's account, the platform takes its cut, then forwards the remainder to the service provider. This routing grants platforms enormous power: they can exploit float, exploit information asymmetry, and exploit bilateral lock-in to extract fees far exceeding the value of their actual service. Platform competition centers on "who can lock in more users" rather than "who provides better service."

In this system, consumer payments are automatically split by the contract and routed directly to the platform, the service provider, and the supplier's respective pools. The platform never touches the full payment. It becomes an ordinary node in the supply chain, collecting a service fee no different in structure from any other supplier. Its competitive edge derives from the quality of its matching service, not from locking in users and fund flows. Because the base layer is an open protocol and switching costs are zero, the maximum fee a platform can charge is the actual value of its matching service.

### 7.10 Real Estate and Housing

Housing is the most vivid example of how lending distorts real economic activity. The mass introduction of mortgage lending structurally transformed the housing market from one naturally regulated by supply and demand into a credit-leverage-driven system where prices detach from real income. Loans amplify every buyer's "ability to pay" simultaneously with credit, inflating prices far beyond what real income supports. Housing shifts from "something you live in" to "an asset you hold," with vast capital flooding into real estate purely to hold and wait for appreciation, crowding out actual construction, because holding is more profitable than building, speculative capital inflates land prices, and developers who want to build new homes face prohibitive land costs.

In this system, lending does not exist, so the first link in this entire distortion chain does not exist. Housing prices can only be supported by real income, with no leverage amplifying purchasing power. Holding real estate does not automatically produce appreciation. Real estate is fully de-financialized. Home purchases no longer go through banks. Buyers and sellers arrange installment payments through escrow pools, similar to a rent-to-own model. If the buyer stops paying midway through, the property is reclaimed; amounts already paid are treated as fair consideration for the period of occupancy. The buyer owes no one anything. No residual debt tail. The total price does not increase with installments, because there is no interest.

### 7.11 Charity and Public Benefit

"Charity" in this system is the same set of primitives, collaboration pools plus escrow pools, configured with a specific set of parameters. The operators form a standard collaboration pool, identical to any other working team. Donors allocate funds to the operating team through escrow pools, setting release conditions, by time, by milestone, or unconditionally. Since charitable operations are non-profit structures, typically configured without incoming revenue streams, donors retain full control over unreleased principal and can withdraw it at any time.

Whenever the operating team changes its distribution rules, every donor has the right to veto: if they disapprove of the new rules, they can withdraw all their unreleased funds. Every donation's destination, every member's share, every distribution , all on-chain and auditable. The problem endemic to traditional charity, that once donated, money enters the institution's pocket and donors have no control over subsequent use, does not exist here, because the institution holding large sums of other people's money does not exist.

### 7.12 Price Discovery

The system does not provide, and does not need, a real-time forward price index generated by a centralized speculative market. Real price information is distributed across every actual transaction and forward agreement.

For standardized commodities requiring public price signals, price discovery is handled by market-driven information services: professional price services form voluntary alliances with their accredited producers. Members contribute real transaction data, anchored by on-chain transactions for authenticity — reported transactions must correspond to an on-chain transfer matching the stated amount. Fabrication requires creating a real transaction and paying tax, making it costly. Members receive the alliance's aggregated real-time pricing in exchange; subscribers who need the signal pay for access. Competing information services vie for subscribers on the basis of accuracy and credibility.

### 7.13 One Variable, Reshaping the Incentives of Any Community

The preceding sections describe changes to specific economic activities, but the reach of this transformation extends far beyond commerce. It touches every community: any structure where people gather around a shared identity, share certain resources, and do certain things together, whether called a nation, a neighborhood, a trade association, or anything else. And what changes them all is, at bottom, a single variable: whether the currency they run on is securitized or relationship-anchored.

Under securitized money, every community faces the same fate: those most skilled at positioning themselves at a control point in the flow of funds gain the upper hand, because securitized money can be transferred and hidden at zero cost — controlling fund flows means being able to extract, and the social cost of extraction is laundered by money's anonymity. Over time, every community drifts toward the same outcome: extractors occupy the core, creators are marginalized, and shared resources are gradually hollowed out. In nations, this manifests as rent-seeking and corruption. In corporations, as bureaucratization and managerial self-dealing. In neighborhoods, as public resources being captured by a few. The names differ; the mechanism is identical.

Anchor the currency in real relationships, and the incentive inverts. Every fund flow is on-chain transparent. Every transfer carries a 20% cost. Controlling fund flows produces no revenue. The only way to capture value is to participate in real economic activity. Value creators automatically hold the advantage; pure extractors are automatically priced out. The 20% transaction tax flows entirely into UBI, and the community's public resource pool fills automatically, beyond anyone's ability to intercept. The more prosperous the community, the higher UBI rises, and the more people want to participate, a positive, self-reinforcing cycle.

This currency does not need to oppose any existing form of community. Nations, neighborhoods, trade associations. These natural forms of human cooperation can continue to exist. What changes is the incentive engine driving them. The same group of people, running on a currency that lets extraction hide, will drift toward corruption and hollowing out. Running on a currency that makes extraction unhideable, they will move toward prosperity and surplus. The people haven't changed. The institutions haven't changed. The only change is whether the currency permits social liabilities to be anonymously hidden, and this single variable, flipped, is sufficient to send the same community's trajectory in a completely opposite direction.

## Chapter 8: Resetting Political Organization

The preceding chapters described how economic activity is reshaped. But money shapes more than economics, it shapes politics. When money's fundamental properties change, political structures inevitably follow.

### 8.1 Political Legitimacy: The Most Direct Casualty of Securitized Money

Chapter 2 analyzed how securitized money hides social liabilities, overdrawn trust, damaged relationships, asymmetrically extracted value. What, precisely, are these hidden "toxic base-layer assets"? They are social trust itself: the healthy relationships among citizens.

Political legitimacy is built on precisely these things. A government's legitimacy ultimately rests on how much mutual trust, how much shared belief in public rules, how much "we are still one community" feeling remains among the people it governs. These are preconditions for a society being governable: when they exist, laws are obeyed, institutions are respected, and disagreements are resolved within the rules. When they do not, any law or institution is just words on paper.

Securitized money's erosion of these base-layer assets is continuous, covert, and irreversible. In a system that structurally rewards anonymous extraction, every successful act of extraction consumes a bit of social trust. , even when those being extracted cannot articulate what is happening, because money's anonymity launders the extraction, they feel that "life keeps getting harder," "the rules aren't fair to me," "I work harder and harder but can afford less and less." This diffuse, sourceless sense of injustice erodes trust in institutions, in rules, in each other — eventually fueling a society-wide anti-establishment sentiment.

This erosion is invisible for long stretches because securitized money maintains the illusion that everything is fine on the surface: GDP is growing, employment numbers are acceptable, financial markets are booming. . When the base-layer trust assets have deteriorated beyond concealment, it surfaces as political crisis: deepening social polarization, rising extremism, pervasive distrust of institutions, populism becoming mainstream. All of these seemingly independent political phenomena are different symptoms of the same underlying condition: social trust, concealed beneath securitized money, has been extracted to the brink of default.

The legitimacy crisis facing a large number of governments worldwide today differs only in pace. It is the inevitable late-stage expression of securitized money structurally eroding social trust. Politics is the most direct casualty of value securitization, because it is the most dependent on the things being eroded.

### 8.2 Government Under Securitized Money: Legitimacy Broker Between Extractors and the Extracted

Under securitized money, society inevitably splits into two camps: those occupying extraction positions, rent-seekers, capital holders, fund-flow controllers, and those being extracted from: workers, consumers, ordinary people without control-point positions. This divide is structural, as analyzed in Chapter 2: it is the inevitable consequence of securitized money allowing extraction to be anonymously hidden.

Government's role in this dynamic is to serve as legitimacy broker between these two camps. On one side, it must make the extracted majority feel that the system is still worth participating in, that some semblance of fairness exists, otherwise society collapses. On the other, it cannot truly threaten the extractors' core interests, because extractors typically command the greatest economic resources and political influence, and, as Chapter 2 showed, the government's own operations depend deeply on the tax revenue and financial infrastructure produced by the securitized structure.

Government is squeezed in the middle. Its work becomes a perpetual balancing act: give the extracted enough comfort and welfare to keep them from flipping the table, while giving extractors enough room to keep extracting, because if extractors withdraw capital, the economy collapses, and the government's fiscal base collapses with it.

This position can be summarized in an old saying: uneasy lies the head that wears a crown. The government sits in this position, bearing pressure from two irreconcilable directions.

### 8.3 Why This Mediation Must Fail

A structural absurdity must be seen clearly: the social liabilities manufactured by securitized money at the economic level, deepening inequality, trust overdraft, hollowed-out communities, purchasing power draining from workers to stockholders, are all pushed to the political level to "solve."

But as analyzed above, political legitimacy itself is built on the very social trust being eroded, politics is this process's most direct victim. Asking the most direct victim to bear the pressure it is suffering and repair the damage it cannot repair. This is the position in which securitized money places government.

This mediation will structurally, inevitably, eventually fail. The reason: it attempts to manage the consequences of the underlying structure without changing that structure, while the consequences continuously worsen until they exceed any capacity for mediation.

Securitized money lets social liabilities be hidden, and hidden social liabilities only accumulate, they never self-resolve. During each boom, extraction accelerates, trust is overdrawn, purchasing power drains from the majority, and unsound economic structures inflate. But because money's anonymity conceals all of this, the surface looks normal. Government appears to be succeeding: the economy grows, tax revenue increases, society remains broadly stable. But beneath the surface, social liabilities accumulate at invisible speed.

When accumulation reaches the tipping point and base-layer defaults begin , manifesting as financial crisis, unemployment waves, or collapse of public trust, government is forced to intervene. Its tools are always the same: deploy more public resources, society's purchasing power, to bail out, stabilize, extend. Each intervention further draws down the government's own credit: debt increases, currency devalues, promises inflate. Because the underlying structure hasn't changed, a new round of hidden accumulation resumes after the crisis. The next crisis will require even larger intervention, even greater overdraft, even deeper credit depletion.

This is a monotonically increasing process: after each cycle, the government's credit balance is lower than before, and the bailout required is larger. Eventually, over decades or generations, the government's credit is exhausted. Currency devaluation spirals out of control. Public services collapse. The social contract disintegrates.

At this point, the mediator has lost the capital to mediate, and the social liabilities accumulated to the limit settle in the most violent fashion. This is the recurring pattern of civilizational crises throughout history, erupting as social collapse, revolution, and war. It is the structural endpoint of "attempting to manage the consequences of a securitized monetary structure through political means without changing the structure." Every crown will eventually shatter under the weight it bears.

We are at such a moment. Across the globe, large numbers of sovereign currencies are rapidly depreciating. Citizens' real purchasing power is shrinking. In a growing number of countries, residents are already using dollar-denominated stablecoins rather than their own national currencies to preserve their savings. Many governments are effectively in a legitimacy crisis. The social liabilities accumulated by the securitized monetary structure have grown too large for any mediation to cover. This is a reality that nearly everyone can feel but very few can articulate the cause of.

### 8.4 The Political Reset Enabled by Relationship-Anchored Money

The relationship-anchored money system will not overthrow existing political order. Local governments retain their sovereignty, continue to make laws, continue to set tax rates and bases, and continue to hold the power of enforcement. What it offers is a directional push: it makes economic activity more transparent, lightens the government's welfare burden, shrinks the need for financial regulation, and makes rent-seeking and corruption harder to hide. What it offers the current political order is the opportunity to choose a future.

Relationship-anchored money changes more than the economy. It changes the deep structure of politics by eliminating the root cause that forces governments into the role of mediator.

The opacity and unaccountability that pervade society trace, at their root, almost entirely to money's function as a mechanism for packaging and transferring social relationships. Over millennia, humanity developed the rule of law, democracy, auditing, regulation, and press freedom — all efforts to make the flow of power and resources trackable and accountable. But these efforts have always been fighting a deeper force: monetary securitization allows every act of acquisition to be anonymously concealed. You can build the most sophisticated regulatory apparatus, but as long as money, once received, can be transferred at zero cost, anonymously, with its origins laundered and its destination severed — accountability will always be chasing a quarry that runs faster.

Relationship-anchored money eliminates this quarry at the base layer: when all value transfers are on-chain transparent, every transfer carries a cost, and every fund can be traced to the real exchange relationship it corresponds to — then the transparency and accountability that humanity has spent millennia trying to achieve through institution-building, for the first time, rests on a structural foundation independent of institutional goodwill.

When social liabilities cannot be hidden, the structural divide between extractors and the extracted ceases to form. People still earn different returns through differences in talent, effort, and judgment; but inequality can only arise from real contribution, not from anonymous extraction. A person who is wealthy, in this system, is someone who has done real things for many people. A person who is poor has participated less in exchange, but still has UBI guaranteeing basic survival. "Extracting continuously from everyone while occupying a control point, without anyone knowing": this becomes structurally impossible.

When that divide ceases to form, government no longer needs to serve as mediator between two camps. It transitions from a role squeezed in the middle , a role that pleases no one and is destined to exhaust its credit, into a clearer, more sustainable role: provider of public services and enforcer of rules.

### 8.5 Government's New Role: Fee-Charging Enforcer and Service Provider

In the relationship-anchored money system, government can and should continue to exist, but its role is fundamentally redefined.

National laws continue to exist and remain effective. Property rights, personal safety, contract enforcement, environmental protection, public infrastructure. These real public services still require someone to provide them. Government is ultimately a collective security organization: a group of people pooling resources to have an institution maintain order and provide public goods that no individual can provide alone. This role does not disappear, but the way it operates becomes fundamentally simpler, more transparent, and more accountable.

Government revenue comes from two clear sources.

First, arbitration service fees: when buyers and sellers designate a government judicial body as the arbitrator in an escrow pool, the arbitration fee is collected by the escrow pool and supports the arbitration and commercial enforcement apparatus. This is a per-case fee-for-service, structurally no different from any other service provider.

Second, taxes: the government, as the collective security and public service organization for a jurisdiction, levies periodic taxes on residents based on a percentage of total personal wallet inflows over a given period , meaning the post-transaction-tax net amount plus UBI income, covering policing, infrastructure, environmental protection, and all other public goods. Every tax item has a clear destination. Every inflow is on-chain and auditable. There is no gray income or hidden income. Tax evaders can be automatically flagged by the system.

Government spending is equally transparent. The government's budget is distributed through collaboration pools to contractors, police departments, court systems, infrastructure builders, public health services, with every expenditure, every contractor's share, and every distribution visible to taxpayers. This level of transparency is impossible for any government under securitized money.

The crucial change: , after stripping away the vast welfare bureaucracy because UBI already handles basic security automatically, the complex financial regulatory apparatus because securitization no longer exists, and the redistributive mechanisms for mediating between extractors and the extracted because the divide no longer forms, the public services government actually needs to provide, namely policing, judiciary, infrastructure, and public health, is a much shorter list than today. This means the effective tax rate required could be far lower than at present.

Government transforms from an ever-expanding machine trying to deploy ever-increasing resources to manage ever-multiplying consequences of securitized money, into a lean, clearly scoped collective security organization, doing less but doing it transparently, spending less but spending where it matters.

### 8.6 The Dissolution of the Social Welfare Burden

Under securitized money, governments carry an enormous social welfare burden, unemployment insurance, pensions, healthcare coverage, housing subsidies, food assistance, because the structural extraction of securitized money continuously manufactures people who need to be bailed out. The more the government backstops, the larger the welfare system grows, and the heavier the fiscal burden becomes, until it becomes a primary force crushing government credit. Moreover, this welfare typically comes with stringent means-testing, requiring people to prove they are poor enough to qualify, which is itself an erosion of human dignity.

In the relationship-anchored money system, UBI performs the core function of social welfare. It provides every person with unconditional baseline survival support. No means-testing, no proof of poverty, no sprawling bureaucracy to administer. This means government can be relieved of most social welfare obligations: no unemployment insurance to manage, because UBI exists; no housing subsidies to manage, because without leverage inflating prices, housing is affordable, supplemented by UBI; no food assistance to manage, because UBI covers basic living expenses. Government fiscal policy is freed from the bottomless pit of ever-expanding welfare spending and can concentrate limited resources on what it actually does well: law enforcement, infrastructure, and public safety.

This achieves the purpose of welfare in a more efficient, more dignified, and less discretion-dependent way. UBI is automatic, equal, and interception-proof. It passes through no bureaucrat's hands, requires no one's approval, and cannot be canceled due to shifts in political winds. It transforms social security from "a government gift to the poor" into "an automatic byproduct of money in motion."

### 8.7 A New Source of Legitimacy

All of this amounts to an exit ramp for government. If it chooses to see it.

The core problem facing a large number of governments today is that their own legitimacy is collapsing from within. Currency depreciation, deteriorating public services, unsustainable welfare systems, crumbling social trust. These are the symptoms of securitized money's accumulated social liabilities reaching their terminal phase. Staying on the old path only brings the endpoint closer.

Relationship-anchored money offers a new source of legitimacy. A government operating within this system's economy gains a fully transparent tax base, a welfare burden largely dissolved by UBI, reduced complexity of financial regulation, and greater difficulty for rent-seeking to hide.

It still levies taxes through legal compulsion, but the rate can be lower because the public expenditure it must cover has shrunk dramatically. Its legitimacy shifts from "whether it can successfully mediate between two camps", a position destined to fail, toward "delivering more transparent public services at lower cost". A more sustainable position. This transition is gradual, directional, and depends on each government's own capacity to choose and adapt.

The degree to which political decision-making is distorted by economic power also decreases. Under securitized money, anonymous large-scale extraction can be frictionlessly converted into political influence. Under relationship-anchored money, economic resources more transparently reflect real contribution, making money's distortion of politics harder to conduct in daylight.

## Chapter 9: When the Exchange Network Collapses

The preceding chapters described the system's internal mechanisms. This chapter analyzes its external raison d'être: why, in the current macroeconomic environment, a network anchored in real exchange relationships may be the only value-bearing structure capable of surviving a base-layer collapse.

### 9.1 The Trust Threshold: The Hidden Premise of Scarcity-Based Money

The entire value proposition of scarcity-based money rests on a premise that is never stated: people want to exchange. Gold and Bitcoin are exchange instruments; their value derives from people's desire to find a convenient, trustworthy securitized instrument to facilitate exchange. When this desire exists, the scarcer the instrument, the more sought after it becomes.

But this desire can vanish. There exists a critical line, a trust threshold. Above this line, social trust and exchange networks function normally, and scarcity serves as an effective medium of value consensus. Below it, people's fear of exchange exceeds their trust in the exchange network, and the entire psychology undergoes a fundamental inversion: when you are not sure whether you can buy food tomorrow, your instinct is to hoard food and exit exchange — to pull resources out of circulation and lock them down, because you have lost confidence in the process of "giving something up and receiving what you need in return." When people are fleeing exchange itself, even the most perfect exchange instrument loses its premise.

This fear is self-reinforcing. Hoarding reduces the supply available for exchange, causing more people to fear, causing more hoarding, reducing supply further. Gold hoarding during the Great Depression was a precise demonstration of this mechanism: the more gold was hoarded as a "store of value," the more liquidity was drained from circulation, and the faster the economy suffocated. Gold's "store of value" function and the economy's "circulation" need were in direct opposition during crisis, and gold served the holder's sense of safety every time, at the cost of the exchange network's death.

The same pattern recurs throughout history whenever exchange networks collapse: people retreat to manor economies, subsistence farming, and barter. Gold's fineness remains impeccable, but it cannot buy a sack of wheat, because no one is willing to trade wheat for a piece of metal.

Most people have never seen the world below the threshold, so they mistake above-threshold experience for eternal law. And , the moments when the narrative failed, when exchange networks collapsed and gold could not buy bread, are too catastrophic and too poorly documented to survive in collective memory. Survivorship bias lets us see only the times gold worked.

### 9.2 What Is Actually Collapsing

The current global exchange network is the post–World War II system of global pricing and exchange, anchored in the US dollar and built on the sovereign credit of nation-states. It is the infrastructure that enables billions of people to exchange across borders. Its underlying credit is being consumed simultaneously by three forces.

Sanctions weaponization. Every instance of turning financial infrastructure into a geopolitical weapon , freezing reserves, severing SWIFT access, sends a signal: this network is one party's power tool. The rational response from other participants is to build parallel networks, de-dollarize, and hoard rather than exchange.

Trade wars. Tariff walls question the fundamental premise: "Why should I accept your rules?" As major economies exclude more and more goods from free exchange under the banner of "national security," the legitimacy consensus underlying the entire network is consumed.

Sovereign debt expansion. Nearly every major economy's sovereign debt-to-GDP ratio is at peacetime historical highs. Each cycle's crisis response involves larger monetary injections and deeper credit overdrafts. The credibility of every node in the network is declining simultaneously.

These three forces form a self-accelerating spiral. Governments chronically overdraw sovereign credit to paper over social liabilities, as analyzed in Chapter 8, causing domestic political credit to erode continuously. When internal contradictions accumulate beyond what can be absorbed, they are externalized, converted into trade protectionism, sanctions, and military adventurism. The dismantling of the external network in turn accelerates internal corrosion: supply-chain disruptions, surging prices for critical goods, and contracting export markets land domestically as higher prices and fewer jobs, further consuming political credit, further creating pressure to externalize. Once initiated, this spiral is self-sustaining.

Market behavior during early 2026 geopolitical conflicts was a warning signal. While oil prices surged over 60%, gold plunged over 18% from historical highs, and Bitcoin crashed within minutes of escalation, triggering hundreds of millions in liquidations. Inflation was surging, and gold was falling, because inflation was merely the symptom. What was actually happening underneath was localized arterial blockage of the exchange network. A single strait, a single commodity, and scarcity-based money failed. If the spiral described in 9.2 deepens further, with multiple supply chains simultaneously severing and multiple critical commodities simultaneously in shortage, the fear of exchange will comprehensively exceed trust. The threshold described in 9.1 will be crossed.

A critically important sequence of events: the contraction of the exchange network is already underway. The collapse of sovereign credit has not yet arrived. Large numbers of developing-world citizens are already preserving savings in dollar stablecoins. The most direct signal of base-layer trust erosion. When the moment of sovereign credit collapse does arrive, it will face a network already riddled with holes. In 1971, the transition was swapping the carrier on an intact network. Next time, the network itself will be too broken for any carrier to hold.

### 9.3 The Only Exit: A Network That Is Still Running

As the external exchange network contracts beyond a certain point, holders of external assets face a simple choice: continue holding assets embedded in a collapsing network, watching their convertibility drain away day by day; or convert them into a network that is still running, restoring real purchasing power.

When the bakeries outside have closed and your gold cannot buy bread, but the bakeries inside are still open and bread costs a 20% tax, you don't need anyone to tell you what to do. The 20% transaction tax, which is a threshold under normal conditions, becomes the entry fee for access to a network that is still functioning in a world where exchange networks are collapsing.

Relationship-anchored money can survive such an environment because it is itself an exchange network. Its value derives from whether real exchange is happening inside it, and this exchange is endogenously driven, independent of any external network's survival. Specifically, when external conditions deteriorate, the system triggers a self-reinforcing positive loop, each stage driven automatically by the structure.

First: UBI generates consumption. Every identity-verified member automatically receives UBI, and consumption is endogenously manufactured. As long as transactions occur, UBI is generated and distributed, and every member has purchasing power. Even if the external exchange network shuts down entirely, the internal consumption cycle continues to run.

Second: external capital enters for real investment. In a stagflationary environment, virtually no asset class in the fiat world offers positive real returns: cash is eaten by inflation, bonds are crushed by rates, equities are hammered by recession, and real estate is steamrolled by deleveraging. Collaboration pool shares offer direct participation in real economic activity, with returns derived from real cash flow, independent of rates, asset prices, or leverage. When the external network has collapsed to the point where "even holding gold, you can't find anyone willing to trade". A network with real internal consumption and services becomes the only positive-return destination for capital. This capital enters through the exchange channel, converting ETH to points, depositing into collaboration pools for shares, thickening the reserve pool, and pushing up the exchange rate.

Third: appreciation amplifies UBI purchasing power. A rising exchange rate means UBI buys more in fiat terms. The same amount of UBI points acquires more real services and goods. Internal consumption expands automatically, requiring no one to drive it.

Fourth: service providers flood in from outside. A market with robust and growing purchasing power attracts external service providers. Skilled people whose fiat-world income is shrinking see strong demand and greater purchasing power in the points world. They enter, provide services, and earn points. Their services generate transactions, transactions generate tax, tax becomes more UBI, and UBI drives more consumption.

Fifth: supply-chain boundaries expand upstream. Service providers earning points inside the system , if points are on an appreciation trajectory, may find that holding for a period yields appreciation sufficient to cover or exceed the 20% boundary cost of purchasing from the fiat world. They are thus incentivized to persuade their upstream suppliers to enter the system as well. Each upstream link that joins eliminates boundary costs for the downstream, while simultaneously thickening the system's internal economic density.

These five stages form a flywheel: consumption drives investment, investment drives appreciation, appreciation attracts supply, supply thickens the economy, and a thicker economy generates more consumption. The worse external conditions become , the faster fiat depreciates, the more the exchange network contracts, the faster the flywheel spins, because the incentive to enter the system grows stronger.

This process has a critical characteristic: it is substantially irreversible. Once a critical mass of real economic activity has migrated inside, participants have no reason to leave. The external network can no longer support any carrier. The participant base only grows, economic density only increases, and every new participant further thickens the network, further pushes up the exchange rate, and further strengthens the pull on the next participant.

This is the relationship-anchored money's true competitive position. It offers something neither gold nor Bitcoin can provide: a living, functioning exchange network. In a world where exchange networks are collapsing, the question is no longer "which currency is more reliable" but "where is exchange still happening." Wherever the answer points, value flows there.

### 9.4 Global Capital Flows and Purchasing-Power Rebalancing

At global scale, the flywheel triggers a profound secondary effect.

In this system, every collaboration pool has complete, on-chain-verifiable revenue records from its very first second of existence. A roasted-corn vendor in Nairobi has a pool displaying the past six months of real revenue, transaction count, and growth trends. An investor in London or New York can review this data, assess whether the business warrants a stake, and directly invest points in exchange for a share of future revenue.

No lawyers, no banks, no intermediaries, no requirement that the vendor understand any legal or financial concepts. Contract rules replace legal protections. On-chain data replaces financial statements. Smart contracts replace intermediaries. In the securitized money world, accessing external capital requires incorporating a company, opening bank accounts, producing compliance documentation, and maintaining auditable financial statements — structurally excluding the vast majority of the world's informal economic activity from capital markets. This system eliminates that barrier entirely.

Investor protection does not rely on legal recourse or asset freezes. It derives from three layers of structural mechanism:

Ex ante, on-chain data provides complete, immutable operating records, enabling investors to make fully informed decisions before committing. Third-party rating services will naturally emerge to analyze and present this data.

During the investment, the investor is a pool shareholder with veto rights over any rule change. Fund exits are hardcoded at creation; no new exit can be added and no distribution ratio changed without the investor's consent. An investor who detects anomalies can halt operations immediately.

Ex post, every wallet is bound to an iris identity, one per person per lifetime, non-cancelable, non-recreatable. An absconding team's entire history remains permanently on-chain. They will never receive investment again in the entire system. In an economy where everyone's income depends on pool participation and investor trust, the permanent destruction of reputation is a stronger deterrent than legal sanction.

This means global capital can flow directly to its highest-return destination. Developed-world capital is in surplus with returns compressed to near-zero. The developing world has abundant high-return opportunities that capital cannot reach, not because good businesses don't exist, but because infrastructure is missing. When an investor in the US earning sub-2% returns sees a well-performing pool in Kenya delivering 15–20% real cash-flow returns, the incentive to deploy capital there is overwhelming.

Their investment strengthens the pool's operating capacity, helping the Kenyan vendor expand. The expanded business generates more transactions and tax, and the tax increases everyone's UBI. Capital pursuing returns, a self-interested act, automatically becomes an engine of global redistribution within this system.

The UBI pool is global. This is another dimension of redistribution. A developer in the US earns 5,000 points; 1,000 points enter the UBI pool. A baker in Kenya earns 100 points; 20 points enter the UBI pool. At distribution, these 1,020 points are divided equally among all members, regardless of country. Tax generated by wealthy-nation participants flows directly into the UBI of developing-world participants.

For a Kenyan, this UBI may approach or exceed local monthly income. For the American, the same amount is pocket change. Purchasing power automatically flows from high-cost regions to low-cost regions, without any government aid allocation, without any bureaucratic intermediary skimming layers, without any political conditions attached. It is the automatic execution of a smart contract. No one can intercept or corrupt it.

This effect self-accelerates. Developing-world participants discover that UBI upon joining the system is highly attractive, and they enter in large numbers. But their entry does not dilute UBI to meaninglessness, because they also participate in economic activity upon arrival, providing services, generating transactions, contributing tax, and expanding the economy's total output. Simultaneously, developed-world consumers discover that services provided by developing-world participants within the system are inexpensive, and investors discover that pools there offer returns far exceeding local opportunities. Both consumption demand and investment capital flow from high-cost to low-cost regions, further boosting incomes for low-cost-region service providers.

Globally, prices for comparable services converge through competition. Every cross-regional transaction contributes 20% back to the UBI pool, benefiting all members. Cross-regional economic activity transforms from a process that can easily create inequality into one that continuously dissolves it.

### 9.5 The Reorganization of Global Economic Order

The global capital flows and purchasing-power rebalancing described above reveal a structural consequence that extends beyond money itself.

The current global economic order is a hierarchy built on a skeleton of debt. Nations at the top hold reserve-currency status and the lowest borrowing costs. Nations at the bottom bear high credit risk ratings and extreme funding costs. The same investment might cost a top-tier nation's firm 3% to finance while a bottom-tier nation's firm pays 15% or more.

This interest-rate differential does not reflect a real productivity gap. It reflects a positional difference within the credit hierarchy, and this position is self-reinforcing: poor, therefore low credit; low credit, therefore expensive capital; expensive capital, therefore unable to develop; unable to develop, therefore still poor. When lending does not exist, "cost of capital" as a concept loses meaning. If all collaboration pools compete for investment on the same level playing field, the basis for competition becomes on-chain data. No longer the irrelevant metric of sovereign credit rating. The developing world's economy steps, in one move, from the periphery of the global economy onto the same table as the developed world.

Current geopolitical confrontation traces, at root, to an intra-sovereign-currency-system contest. Reserve-currency status enables power projection through financial sanctions and capital market advantages. Other major powers seek to challenge this order by building alternative payment networks and regional currency arrangements. But these alternatives are the same species as what they seek to replace, all are sovereign credit currencies, all competing over "whose security is more trusted."

An economic network independent of any sovereign credit does not participate in this contest, it routes around it. As more economic activity occurs within such a network, the question "whose currency is the reserve currency" gradually loses relevance. De-globalization is similarly bypassed: tariffs, sanctions, and technology blockades can obstruct flows on the old network; they cannot obstruct flows on a borderless, unfreezable network that depends on no government infrastructure. Globalization has not been reversed, it has changed carriers.

What blockchain technology can truly do is enable a set of economic rules, independent of any trusted institution, to be faithfully executed worldwide. If the system described in this whitepaper can move from concept to reality, it would represent humanity's first attempt to reorganize the global economic order through code rather than violence.

### 9.6 Ethereum and the System's Positive Feedback Loop

This system runs as smart contracts on Ethereum, with ETH as the sole reserve. The choice was justified in Section 5.4 from the perspectives of security and unfreezability. But it also carries a deeper structural consequence: a self-accelerating positive feedback loop between the system's success and ETH's value.

Since Ethereum's transition to proof of stake in 2022, a portion of gas fees consumed by each transaction is permanently burned. Higher transaction volume means more ETH burned, faster supply contraction. All economic activity in this system occurs on Ethereum, every point transfer, every collaboration pool distribution, every exchange channel entry and exit consumes gas and burns ETH. The more successful the system, the greater the transaction volume, and the stronger the deflationary pressure on ETH.

Simultaneously, external capital entering the system must convert ETH to points through the exchange channel — meaning the system's expansion generates continuous, structural buy pressure on ETH in the open market. The reserve pool's ETH increases in quantity through inflows and increases in unit price through deflation, two dimensions expanding simultaneously. The growing market value of the reserve pool directly pushes up the external purchasing power of points, and therefore of every participant's UBI. Enhanced purchasing power attracts more service providers and capital into the system. Entry generates more transactions, more transactions consume more gas and draw more ETH into the reserve. The cycle accelerates.

This positive feedback is especially critical during cold start. The system's greatest early challenge is establishing points' external purchasing power and attracting the first service providers. ETH as the reserve provides a natural external accelerator: Ethereum has the largest developer community in crypto. These developers mostly hold ETH and can immediately see the alignment between this system and ETH's value. They have the ability to audit and implement the code, the incentive to drive the project's success because it directly strengthens the value of the ETH they hold, and near-zero cost of participation because everything is open source and runs on their most familiar chain.

External speculative volatility in ETH is also asymmetrically filtered by this structure. When ETH is bid up externally, the reserve pool appreciates, and system participants' UBI purchasing power automatically increases. The upside transmits inward. When ETH crashes externally, internal prices adjust upward to reflect reduced external purchasing power, but the underlying real economic activity continues to run: people still transact, services are still provided, collaboration pools still distribute income.

This network of real exchange relationships gives ETH a buffer that the speculative structures of the securitized money world cannot provide: price volatility is absorbed by real economic activity rather than amplified by leverage and panic as in speculative markets. Internal transaction volume does not vanish because ETH's price drops; gas continues to be consumed, ETH continues to be burned, forming a price floor. The system functionally becomes a stabilizer for ETH, providing it with a layer of support anchored in real economic activity.
